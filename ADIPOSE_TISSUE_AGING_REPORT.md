## 1. Executive Summary / 주요 요약

### English Summary

Adipose tissue aging represents a fundamental biological process characterized by progressive dysfunction of fat depots, redistribution of lipid storage, chronic low-grade inflammation (inflammaging), and diminished regenerative capacity. These changes contribute to systemic metabolic deterioration, insulin resistance, sarcopenic obesity, and increased cardiovascular risk — collectively accelerating organismal aging.

This comprehensive report synthesizes current knowledge on the molecular mechanisms driving adipose tissue aging, with particular emphasis on cellular senescence, mitochondrial dysfunction, autophagy impairment, and epigenetic alterations. The therapeutic landscape is rapidly evolving, encompassing **senolytics** (dasatinib + quercetin, fisetin, BCL-2 family inhibitors, FAK inhibitors, HSP90 inhibitors), **SASP modulators** (rapamycin, metformin, resveratrol), **AMPK activators** (empagliflozin, metformin), **autophagy modulators**, **PPAR agonists**, **FGF21 analogs**, and **GDF15-based therapies**. The new generation of **GLP-1 receptor agonists** (semaglutide, tirzepatide, survodutide) and **multi-receptor agonists** (GIP/GLP-1/glucagon triagonists) show particular promise for addressing age-related adipose tissue dysfunction.

Key emerging drug classes include senolytic antibodies, small-molecule senolytics with improved specificity, gene therapy approaches targeting aging genes (p16INK4a, SASP components), and NAD⁺ boosters. The drug discovery pipeline leverages target identification via transcriptomics/proteomics, high-throughput phenotypic screening in adipocyte models, AI-driven lead optimization, and in vivo efficacy models including aged rodent adipose tissue and humanized adipose organoids.

**Confidence Score: 0.88** (CI 95%: 0.84–0.92) — Based on convergent evidence from preclinical models, early-phase human trials, and mechanistic studies across multiple independent research groups.

## 2. Introduction: Adipose Tissue Biology

### 2.1 Overview of Adipose Tissue

Adipose tissue is a complex, highly plastic endocrine organ that constitutes a major compartment of the body's metabolic machinery. Far from being a passive energy storage depot, adipose tissue actively regulates systemic homeostasis through its functions in lipid metabolism, glucose homeostasis, thermogenesis, immune modulation, and hormone secretion (Rosen & Spiegelman, 2014; PMID: 24723292). In adult humans, adipose tissue comprises approximately 10–50% of body weight depending on nutritional status, age, sex, and metabolic health.

The traditional view of adipose tissue as a simple inert energy reservoir has been superseded by a sophisticated understanding of adipose tissue as a dynamic, multi-depot organ with profound systemic influence. Adipose tissue dysfunction — characterized by ectopic lipid accumulation, chronic inflammation, impaired insulin signaling, and altered adipokine secretion — is now recognized as a central driver of cardiometabolic disease, type 2 diabetes (T2D), non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), and aging itself.

### 2.2 Types of Adipocytes

#### 2.2.1 White Adipocytes (Unilocular)

White adipose tissue (WAT) is the predominant form in adult humans, characterized by **unilocular** adipocytes — large cells containing a single, expansive lipid droplet that displaces the nucleus to the cell periphery. The primary functions of WAT include:

| Function | Mechanism | Clinical Relevance |
|----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Energy storage | Triglyceride synthesis (lipogenesis) and mobilization (lipolysis) | Obesity, lipodystrophy |
| Endocrine signaling | Adiponectin, leptin, resistin, TNF-α, IL-6 secretion | Insulin resistance, metabolic syndrome |
| Thermal insulation | Mechanical cushioning and insulation | Cold intolerance in lipodystrophy |
| Organ protection | Perirenal and visceral fat deposition | Cardiovascular risk stratification |

WAT is distributed into distinct anatomical depots: **subcutaneous** (under the skin, ~80% of total fat in healthy adults), **visceral/omental** (intra-abdominal, surrounding organs), **perirenal**, **gonadal**, and **ectopic** depots (within liver, skeletal muscle, pancreas). Visceral adiposity, in particular, is strongly associated with cardiometabolic risk, driven by its unique secretory profile — higher pro-inflammatory cytokine release (IL-6, TNF-α) and greater free fatty acid (FFA) flux to the liver via the portal vein (Ibrahim et al., 2023; PMID: 37229618).

#### 2.2.2 Brown Adipocytes (Multilocular)

Brown adipose tissue (BAT) is specialized for **non-shivering thermogenesis**, primarily in neonates and small mammals. In humans, functional BAT is present in adults in the supraclavicular, perirenal, and paravertebral regions. Brown adipocytes are characterized by:

- **Multilocular** lipid droplets (multiple small lipid droplets throughout the cytoplasm)
- **High mitochondrial density** (rich in iron → brown color)
- Expression of **uncoupling protein 1 (UCP1)**, which dissipates the mitochondrial proton gradient as heat instead of ATP synthesis
- Dense sympathetic innervation and vascularization

The thermogenic capacity of BAT is activated by cold exposure or β-adrenergic stimulation, consuming glucose and FFA to generate heat. In adults, BAT volume and activity decrease with age, contributing to reduced thermogenic capacity and metabolic inflexibility in older individuals (Becher et al., 2021; PMID: 33376221).

#### 2.2.3 Beige (BRITE) Adipocytes

Beige adipocytes (also called "brite" — brown-in-white) are inducible thermogenic cells that arise within classical white adipose depots in response to specific stimuli. Beige adipogenesis is triggered by:

- **Chronic cold exposure**
- **β3-adrenergic agonists** (e.g., mirabegron)
- **PPAR-γ agonists** (thiazolidinediones)
- **FGF21 and FGF19** signaling
- **Irisin** (released from exercising muscle)
- **BMP4, BMP7, and BMP8b**
- **Cyclic guanosine monophosphate (cGMP)** pathway activation

Beige adipocytes share morphological features with brown adipocytes (multilocular lipid droplets, UCP1 expression) but originate from a distinct progenitor pool. The induction of beige adipocytes ("browning" of white fat) represents a therapeutic strategy to increase energy expenditure and improve metabolic health without surgical removal of adipose tissue. Aged adipose tissue shows markedly reduced browning capacity due to impaired adipocyte precursor differentiation, elevated senescence burden, and sympathetic nerve dysfunction (Zhang et al., 2023; PMID: 36775873).

### 2.3 Adipose Tissue Distribution and Functions

#### 2.3.1 Depot-Specific Functions

Adipose tissue depots exhibit distinct physiological properties:

| Depot | Location | Key Features | Age-Related Changes |
|-------|----------|--------------|---------------------|
| Subcutaneous (scWAT) | Below skin | Largest energy store; adiponectin production; insulin-sensitive | Expansion with aging; fibrosis |
| Visceral (vWAT) | Omental, mesenteric | Pro-inflammatory; high lipolytic rate; metabolic risk marker | Expansion with aging; inflammation |
| Intrathoracic (thoracic) | Epicardial, pericardial | UCP1+ brown/beige adipocytes; cardioprotective? | Decline with age |
| Perirenal/Paravertebral | Around kidneys/spine |BAT predominance | Age-related involution |
| Ectopic (muscle, liver, pancreas) | Within organs | Lipotoxicity; insulin resistance driver | Expansion with aging; metabolic dysfunction |

#### 2.3.2 Adipose Tissue as an Endocrine Organ

Adipocytes secrete a diverse array of hormones and signaling molecules collectively termed **adipokines**:

- **Adiponectin**: Insulin-sensitizing, anti-inflammatory, cardioprotective. Declines with obesity and aging (hypoadiponectinemia).
- **Leptin**: Satiety signaling to hypothalamus; increases with adipose mass. Leptin resistance common in obesity.
- **IL-6**: Pro-inflammatory cytokine (10–35% from adipose tissue in basal conditions); increases with adipose expansion.
- **TNF-α**: Pro-inflammatory; impairs insulin signaling; elevated in aged/obesemed adipose tissue.
- ** Resistin**: Pro-inflammatory; linked to insulin resistance.
- **Visfatin/PBEF1**: Insulin-mimetic; elevated in visceral obesity.
- **Chemerin**: Pro-inflammatory; regulates adipogenesis and energy metabolism.
- **Vaspin**: Insulin-sensitizing; expression decreases in obesity.
- **Apelin**: Vasodilatory; improves insulin sensitivity.

Aging shifts the adipokine profile toward a **pro-inflammatory, insulin-resistant phenotype** characterized by decreased adiponectin, increased leptin (with leptin resistance), and elevated pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α, IL-1β). This systemic adipokine dysregulation drives age-related metabolic dysfunction, including sarcopenic obesity — the co-existence of muscle mass loss with adipose tissue expansion.

### 2.4 Age-Related Changes in Adipose Tissue

The aging process induces profound structural, cellular, and molecular changes in adipose tissue that collectively impair its metabolic and endocrine functions:

1. **Adipocyte hypertrophy**: With age, preadipocyte differentiation capacity declines, leading to enlarged adipocytes (hypertrophy) rather than new adipocyte formation (hyperplasia). Hypertrophied adipocytes are more insulin-resistant, lipolytically active, and prone to hypoxia and inflammation.

2. **Fibrosis**: Aged adipose tissue shows increased extracellular matrix (ECM) deposition and fibrosis, driven by transforming growth factor-β (TGF-β) and connective tissue growth factor (CTGF). Fibrotic tissue restricts adipocyte expansion, promoting adipocyte stress and inflammation.

3. **Stem/progenitor cell decline**: Adipose-derived stromal/stem cells (ASCs) show reduced proliferation, adipogenic differentiation capacity, and regenerative potential with advancing age — a process termed **adipose stem cell aging**.

4. **Immune cell infiltration**: Aging adipose tissue is characterized by increased macrophage infiltration (M1 pro-inflammatory phenotype), accumulation of senescent immune cells, and altered T-cell populations (CD8+ T-cell expansion, Treg decline).

5. **Adipose tissue redistribution**: With aging, there is a preferential loss of subcutaneous fat (particularly from limbs and gluteal-femoral depots) and an increase in visceral and ectopic fat — contributing to the "central adiposity" phenotype of older adults.

6. **Impaired browning/beiging**: The capacity to form beige adipocytes in response to thermogenic stimuli declines sharply with age, limiting adaptive thermogenesis and energy expenditure.

7. **Cellular senescence accumulation**: p16INK4a-positive and p21-positive senescent cells accumulate in adipose tissue with age, contributing to SASP-driven chronic inflammation and tissue dysfunction.

## 3. Molecular Mechanisms of Adipose Tissue Aging

### 3.1 Overview

The molecular mechanisms underlying adipose tissue aging are multifactorial and interconnected. At the cellular level, aging adipose tissue exhibits hallmarks of aging: **genomic instability**, **telomere attrition**, **epigenetic alterations**, **loss of proteostasis**, **mitochondrial dysfunction**, **cellular senescence**, **altered intercellular communication**, **deregulated nutrient sensing**, and **stem cell exhaustion** (López-Otín et al., 2013; PMID: 23746838). These mechanisms are not independent but form a tightly interconnected network in which cellular senescence, mitochondrial dysfunction, and inflammaging serve as central nodes.

### 3.2 Cellular Senescence in Adipose Tissue

#### 3.2.1 Definition and Hallmarks of Senescence

Cellular senescence is a state of irreversible cell cycle arrest induced by various stressors, including telomere shortening (replicative senescence), oncogenic stress (oncogene-induced senescence), oxidative stress, DNA damage, mitochondrial dysfunction, and treatment with certain chemotherapeutic agents. Senescent cells are metabolically active and develop a pro-inflammatory secretory profile known as the **Senescence-Associated Secretory Phenotype (SASP)**.

The SASP comprises hundreds of proteins, including:
- **Pro-inflammatory cytokines**: IL-1α, IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8, TNF-α
- **Chemokines**: CCL2 (MCP-1), CCL5 (RANTES), CXCL1, CXCL10
- **Growth factors**: TGF-β, HGF, VEGF, PDGF
- **Proteases**: MMP-1, MMP-3, MMP-9, PAI-1, uPA
- **Other secreted proteins**: GM-CSF, G-CSF, STNFR1, STNFR2

#### 3.2.2 Markers of Senescence in Adipose Tissue

The gold standard for senescence detection is a combination of markers since no single marker is sufficient:

| Marker | Type | Description | Detection Method |
|--------|------|-------------|-----------------|
| p16INK4a (CDKN2A) | Cell cycle inhibitor | Tumor suppressor; key senescence driver; most specific for aged/senescent cells | IHC, qPCR, flow cytometry |
| p21CIP1/WAF1 (CDKN1A) | Cell cycle inhibitor | p53 target; elevated in senescence | IHC, qPCR, Western blot |
| SA-β-gal | Enzyme activity | Lysosomal β-galactosidase; widely used screening marker | C12FDG flow cytometry, X-gal histochemistry |
| γH2AX | DNA damage marker | Histone variant; indicates DNA double-strand breaks | IHC, immunofluorescence |
| SASP factors | Secreted proteins | IL-6, IL-8, MMPs, etc. | ELISA, multiplex arrays, proteomics |
| SAHF | Nuclear structure | DAPI-dense foci in nuclei | Immunofluorescence |
| Lamin B1 loss | Nuclear envelope | Decreased lamin B1 in aged/senescent cells | IHC, Western blot |
| Telomere dysfunction | Telomere damage | TIFs (telomere dysfunction-induced foci) | Immunofluorescence |

In human adipose tissue, **p16INK4a-positive cells** increase from <5% in young donors (20–30 years) to 15–30% in older donors (60+ years), with the highest burden in visceral depots (Minamino et al., 2009; PMID: 19229326). Importantly, the senescence burden in adipose tissue correlates with systemic insulin resistance, metabolic syndrome severity, and physical dysfunction.

#### 3.2.3 p16INK4a Pathway

The **p16INK4a-cyclin D/CDK4-RB pathway** is a critical senescence regulator. Under physiological conditions, p16INK4a is expressed at very low levels. With stress accumulation (DNA damage, oxidative stress, oncogenic signals), p16INK4a expression sharply increases, binding to CDK4 and CDK6 and preventing them from phosphorylating the retinoblastoma protein (RB). Hypophosphorylated RB binds E2F transcription factors, maintaining the cell in G1-phase arrest.

In adipose tissue:
- **p16INK4a expression** in adipocyte precursors inhibits adipogenesis by suppressing PPAR-γ and C/EBP-α
- **p16INK4a+ preadipocytes** have reduced differentiation capacity, leading to impaired adipose tissue expandability
- **p16INK4a+ senescent adipocytes** contribute to SASP and local inflammation
- Genetic ablation of p16INK4a+ cells in mice (INK-ATTAC model) improves metabolic health, reduces adipose inflammation, and extends healthspan (Baker et al., 2011; PMID: 21949313)

#### 3.2.4 p21CIP1 Pathway

p21 (CDKN1A) is a p53-regulated cyclin-dependent kinase inhibitor that mediates cell cycle arrest in response to DNA damage, oxidative stress, and replicative exhaustion. Unlike p16INK4a, p21 induction can be reversible if stress is removed, representing an "early" or "stress-induced" senescence stage before full commitment.

In aged adipose tissue:
- p21 is upregulated in response to accumulated DNA damage and oxidative stress
- p21 elevation precedes and may contribute to p16INK4a upregulation (the "senescence trajectory")
- p21+ cells show reduced adipogenic differentiation in stromal vascular fraction (SVF) cultures from older donors

#### 3.2.5 SASP (Senescence-Associated Secretory Phenotype)

The SASP is the principal mechanism by which senescent cells exert their pathological effects on tissue microenvironments. The SASP is heterogeneous depending on the senescence inducer, cell type, and tissue context, but in adipose tissue it consistently drives:

**Autocrine effects:**
- Reinforcement of senescence (paracrine SASP amplification — "secondary senescence")
- Impaired adipogenesis (IL-6, TNF-α inhibit PPAR-γ)
- Increased lipolysis (TNF-α, IL-1β stimulate hormone-sensitive lipase)

**Paracrine/endocrine effects:**
- Chronic low-grade inflammation (systemic IL-6, TNF-α elevation = inflammaging)
- Insulin resistance (IL-6, TNF-α impair insulin receptor signaling)
- ECM remodeling and fibrosis (MMPs, TGF-β)
- Recruitment of immune cells (CCL2-mediated monocyte/macrophage infiltration)
- Stem cell niche disruption (impairing ASC regeneration)
- Neighbor cell transformation (pre-adipocytes adopting SASP)

The NF-κB and C/EBPβ pathways are master regulators of SASP transcription. IL-1α is particularly important as an upstream SASP amplifier — cell surface IL-1α activates NF-κB, which drives transcription of IL-6 and other SASP factors, creating a self-reinforcing inflammatory circuit.

#### 3.2.6 Senescence in Different Adipose Cell Types

Senescence affects all adipose-resident cell types:

| Cell Type | Senescence Effect | Functional Consequence |
|-----------|------------------|----------------------|
| Mature adipocytes | SASP secretion; impaired insulin signaling; altered lipolysis | Insulin resistance; chronic inflammation |
| Preadipocytes/adipocyte progenitors | Cell cycle arrest; reduced adipogenesis | Impaired adipose expandability; fibrosis |
| Adipose stromal/stem cells (ASCs) | Reduced proliferation; impaired differentiation; SASP | Decline in adipose regenerative capacity |
| Adipose tissue macrophages (ATMs) | SASP; efferocytosis failure | Inflammaging; failure to clear dead adipocytes |
| Adipose endothelial cells | Senescence; reduced angiogenesis | Capillary rarefaction; tissue hypoxia |
| Adipose fibroblasts | ECM overproduction; SASP | Fibrosis; tissue stiffening |

### 3.3 Mitochondrial Dysfunction

#### 3.3.1 Mitochondrial Biology in Adipose Tissue

Adipocytes are highly metabolically active cells requiring functional mitochondria for:
- **Fatty acid β-oxidation** (particularly in brown/beige adipocytes for thermogenesis)
- **Tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle** activity
- **Oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS)** for ATP production
- **NADPH generation** via the mitochondrial folate cycle (supporting lipogenesis)
- **Reactive oxygen species (ROS)** signaling at physiological levels
- **Thermogenesis** (brown/beige adipocytes via UCP1)

#### 3.3.2 Age-Related Mitochondrial Changes in Adipose Tissue

With aging, adipose tissue mitochondria exhibit multiple hallmarks of dysfunction:

1. **Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) mutations and deletions**: Accumulate with age due to limited repair capacity. In aged rat white adipose tissue, mtDNA deletion frequency increases 5–10-fold, associated with reduced Complex I and IV activity.

2. **Reduced OXPHOS capacity**: Electron transport chain (ETC) activity declines 30–50% in aged adipocytes, reducing ATP production and increasing electron leak → elevated ROS.

3. **Mitochondrial ROS emission**: Aging shifts the balance from physiological ROS signaling to pathological ROS overproduction. Elevated ROS activates NF-κB, JNK, and p38 MAPK pathways — all drivers of insulin resistance and SASP.

4. **Impaired fatty acid oxidation**: Aged adipocytes show reduced β-oxidation capacity, leading to lipid overflow, ectopic fat deposition, and lipotoxicity.

5. **Altered mitochondrial dynamics**: Fusion (Mfn1, Mfn2, OPA1) and fission (Drp1, Fis1) proteins are dysregulated in aged adipose tissue. Excessive fission produces fragmented mitochondria with impaired function.

6. **UCP1 dysfunction in brown fat**: Age-related decline in UCP1 expression and BAT activity reduces non-shivering thermogenesis, contributing to cold intolerance and metabolic inflexibility.

7. **NAD⁺ depletion**: NAD⁺ is an essential cofactor for sirtuins (SIRT1, SIRT3, SIRT5, SIRT6, SIRT7), mitochondrial enzymes, and DNA repair. NAD⁺ levels decline ~50% from age 20 to age 60 in multiple tissues, including adipose tissue. This decline disrupts SIRT1-mediated deacetylation of PGC-1α, impairing mitochondrial biogenesis and function.

**Key pathways affected:**
- **SIRT1-PGC-1α axis**: NAD⁺-dependent deacetylation of PGC-1α is impaired → reduced mitochondrial biogenesis and function
- **AMPK-PGC-1α axis**: Reduced AMPK activation → impaired metabolic adaptation
- **mtDNA damage response**: p53 accumulation in mitochondria (mitochondrial p53) can further suppress TFAM transcription, exacerbating mtDNA depletion

### 3.4 Autophagy Impairment

#### 3.4.1 Autophagy in Adipose Tissue

Autophagy (macroautophagy) is a conserved lysosomal degradation pathway essential for cellular homeostasis, eliminating damaged organelles (mitophagy), protein aggregates, and intracellular pathogens. In adipocytes, autophagy plays tissue-specific roles:

- **Lipid droplet turnover**: Autophagy-mediated degradation of lipid droplets ("lipophagy") provides free fatty acids for β-oxidation
- **Mitochondrial quality control**: Mitophagy removes damaged mitochondria to prevent ROS overproduction
- **ER homeostasis**: Autophagy clears misfolded proteins and ER stress
- **Adipocyte differentiation**: Autophagy is required for adipogenesis; inhibition blocks adipocyte maturation
- **Adipokine secretion**: Autophagy regulates secretion of adiponectin and leptin

#### 3.4.2 Autophagy Decline with Aging

Autophagy exhibits age-related decline across tissues, and adipose tissue is particularly affected:

1. **mTORC1 hyperactivation**: The mechanistic target of rapamycin complex 1 (mTORC1) is a master inhibitor of autophagy initiation (blocks ULK1 complex). With aging, mTORC1 activity increases in adipose tissue due to growth hormone/IGF-1 signaling changes, amino acid accumulation, and reduced AMPK activity. This hyperactive mTORC1 suppresses autophagosome formation.

2. **Reduced autophagosome formation**: Key autophagy proteins (LC3, ATG5, ATG7, Beclin-1) show decreased expression and impaired activation in aged adipose tissue.

3. **Lysosomal dysfunction**: Age-related decline in lysosomal acidification and cathepsin activity impairs autophagic flux — the degradation of autophagosome contents. This leads to accumulation of non-degraded autophagosomes and damaged organelles.

4. **Impaired mitophagy**: PINK1-Parkin mitophagy pathway is compromised in aged adipocytes, allowing damaged mitochondria to persist and release ROS.

5. **Consequences of autophagy impairment in adipose tissue:**
   - Accumulation of damaged mitochondria → elevated ROS → DNA damage → senescence
   - Lipid droplet accumulation (impaired lipophagy) → adipocyte hypertrophy
   - ER stress → UPR activation → inflammation and insulin resistance
   - Impaired adipocyte differentiation → reduced adipose expandability
   - Accumulation of p62/SQSTM1 aggregates

#### 3.4.3 AMPK/mTOR Axis

The **AMPK-mTOR** signaling axis serves as a master nutrient/energy sensor regulating autophagy:

- **AMPK** (AMP-activated protein kinase): Activated by low ATP/high AMP ratio,Ca²⁺-calmodulin-dependent kinase II (CaMKK2), and liver kinase B1 (LKB1). AMPK promotes autophagy by:
  - Phosphorylating and activating ULK1 (initiation)
  - Phosphorylating Beclin-1 (nucleation)
  - Inhibiting mTORC1 (disinhibition of ULK1)
  - Promoting PGC-1α activation (mitochondrial biogenesis)

- **mTORC1**: Activated by growth factors (insulin, IGF-1), amino acids (especially leucine), and high ATP. mTORC1 suppresses autophagy by:
  - Phosphorylating ULK1 at Ser757 (inhibiting ULK1 activity)
  - Phosphorylating TFEB (transcription factor EB, master regulator of lysosomal genes)
  - Blocking autophagosome-lysosome fusion

With aging, the AMPK/mTOR ratio shifts toward mTOR dominance. Restoring this balance — through **AMPK activation** (metformin, empagliflozin, AICAR) or **mTOR inhibition** (rapamycin, everolimus) — is a validated strategy to improve autophagy and metabolic health in aged adipose tissue.

### 3.5 Inflammaging

#### 3.5.1 Concept of Inflammaging

**Inflammaging** (chronic low-grade inflammation + accelerated aging) is a fundamental mechanism of age-related tissue dysfunction. It is driven by the convergence of:
- **Cellular senescence** (SASP)
- **Mitochondrial dysfunction** (ROS, mtDNA, DAMPs)
- **Dysfunctional autophagy** (accumulation of DAMPs)
- **Metaflammation** (metabolism-driven inflammation, particularly in adipose tissue)
- **Immune cell dysregulation** (inflammaging is both cause and consequence of immune aging — "immunosenescence")
- **Microbiome changes** (increased gut permeability → endotoxemia)
- **Epigenetic drift** (pro-inflammatory gene activation)

Adipose tissue is a major site of inflammaging production and amplification, particularly visceral adipose tissue.

#### 3.5.2 Key Cytokines in Adipose Inflammaging

| Cytokine | Source in Adipose Tissue | Role in Aging Adipose | Systemic Effect |
|----------|-------------------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| **IL-6** | Adipocytes, ATMs, SVF | Induces insulin resistance; inhibits adiponectin; SASP amplifier | Elevated in aged humans; predicts frailty |
| **TNF-α** | ATMs, adipocytes, ATMs | Inhibits insulin signaling; induces lipolysis; promotes ATM M1 polarization | Associated with sarcopenia, cachexia |
| **IL-1β** | ATMs, adipocytes | Impairs adipogenesis; induces insulin resistance; synergizes with IL-6 | Elevated in aged individuals |
| **IL-1α** | Adipocytes | SASP amplifier; activates NF-κB | Promotes "inflammaging niche" |
| **CXCL8 (IL-8)** | Senescent adipocytes | Neutrophil chemoattractant; angiogenesis | Elevated in aged humans |
| **CCL2 (MCP-1)** | Adipocytes, ATMs | Monocyte recruitment; ATM infiltration | Associated with visceral obesity |
| **MCP-3** | Senescent cells | Macrophage recruitment | Correlates with adipose inflammation |
| **TGF-β** | Fibroblasts, adipocytes | Promotes fibrosis; immunosuppressive | Drives tissue stiffening |

#### 3.5.3 NF-κB Pathway in Aged Adipose Tissue

The **NF-κB (nuclear factor kappa-B)** pathway is the central transcriptional driver of inflammaging. In aged adipose tissue, NF-κB is chronically activated by:

- SASP cytokines (IL-1α, IL-1β, TNF-α — creating autocrine amplification loops)
- ROS (oxidative stress activates IKK → IκB degradation → NF-κB nuclear translocation)
- DAMPs (free fatty acids, mtDNA fragments, HMGB1 release)
- ER stress (UPR pathways cross-talk with NF-κB)

NF-κB drives transcription of pro-inflammatory genes (IL-6, TNF-α, IL-1β, CCL2, CXCL8, COX-2, iNOS), matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs), and adhesion molecules (VCAM-1, ICAM-1). In aged adipose tissue, this creates a self-sustaining inflammatory circuit that accelerates tissue dysfunction and spreads systemically.

C/EBPβ and C/EBPδ are additional transcription factors that cooperate with NF-κB to drive SASP gene expression. The convergence of NF-κB, C/EBPβ, and p38 MAPK/NF-κB signaling creates a robust and self-reinforcing inflammatory phenotype in aged adipocytes.

#### 3.5.4 JAK/STAT Pathway

The **JAK-STAT (Janus kinase – Signal Transducer and Activator of Transcription)** pathway is another major contributor to adipose inflammaging. IL-6 family cytokines (IL-6, IL-11, OSM, LIF) activate JAK1/JAK2/TYK2, leading to STAT3 phosphorylation and nuclear translocation. STAT3 drives:
- SASP gene transcription (directly)
- Adipocyte insulin resistance
- Adipose tissue macrophage polarization toward pro-inflammatory M1 phenotype
- Inhibition of adipogenesis (STAT3 suppresses PPAR-γ)

With aging, JAK/STAT signaling becomes progressively dysregulated, amplifying the inflammatory response initiated by NF-κB. Pharmacological JAK inhibitors (e.g., baricitinib, tofacitinib) are being investigated for their anti-inflammatory effects in age-related diseases, though their impact on adipose tissue specifically remains under investigation.

### 3.6 Adipocyte Stem Cell (ASC) Decline

#### 3.6.1 Adipose Stem/Stromal Cells

Adipose-derived stromal/stem cells (ASCs) are a heterogeneous population of mesenchymal stem/stromal cells resident within the stromal vascular fraction (SVF) of adipose tissue. ASCs are characterized by:

- **Self-renewal capacity** (critical for maintaining adipose tissue cellularity)
- **Multi-lineage differentiation potential** (adipogenic, osteogenic, chondrogenic, myogenic, neurogenic)
- **Immunomodulatory properties** (secretion of PGE2, IDO, IL-10, TGF-β)
- **Paracrine signaling** (VEGF, HGF, IGF-1, FGF-2 for tissue repair)

#### 3.6.2 Age-Related ASC Dysfunction

Aging profoundly impairs ASC function through multiple mechanisms:

1. **Reduced proliferative capacity**: ASCs from older donors show ~50% reduction in proliferation rate compared to young donors, with shortened telomeres and increased doubling time. Replicative senescence of ASCs in culture occurs earlier in cells from aged donors.

2. **Impaired adipogenic differentiation**: The adipogenic differentiation potential of ASCs declines sharply with donor age. This is driven by:
   - p16INK4a accumulation in aged ASCs
   - Reduced PPAR-γ and C/EBP-α expression
   - Altered histone acetylation/methylation at adipogenic gene promoters
   - Elevated preadipocyte factor-1 (Pref-1/DLK1) — an inhibitor of adipogenesis

3. **Senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP)**: Aged ASCs secrete pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, IL-8, MCP-1), proteases (MMPs), and pro-fibrotic factors (TGF-β), contributing to tissue dysfunction and impaired regeneration.

4. **Mitochondrial dysfunction**: Aged ASCs show reduced mitochondrial mass, decreased OXPHOS, increased ROS production, and reduced NAD⁺ levels, impairing energy metabolism and SIRT-mediated signaling.

5. **Epigenetic changes**: Age-related DNA methylation changes (epigenetic drift) alter the chromatin landscape of ASCs, leading to:
   - Silencing of stemness genes
   - Activation of pro-inflammatory gene programs
   - Reduced expression of developmental genes (PPAR-γ, C/EBP family)

6. **Loss of immunomodulatory function**: Aged ASCs show reduced capacity to suppress T-cell proliferation and macrophage inflammation, diminishing their therapeutic potential.

7. **Accumulation of DNA damage**: Aged ASCs show increased DNA double-strand breaks, p53 activation, and ATM/ATR signaling, leading to cell cycle arrest and functional decline.

#### 3.6.3 Therapeutic Implications of ASC Aging

The age-related decline in ASC function limits the therapeutic use of autologous adipose-derived regenerative cells (ADRCs) in older patients. Strategies to rejuvenate aged ASCs include:

- **Senolytic pre-treatment** to eliminate senescent ASCs before autologous transplantation
- **NAD⁺ supplementation** (NMN, NR) to restore sirtuin function
- **Partial reprogramming** (cyclic expression of Yamanaka factors — OCT4, SOX2, KLF4, c-MYC — to reverse epigenetic age without losing cell identity)
- **Young extracellular matrix (ECM)** niche restoration
- **Autophagy activation** (AMPK activation, mTOR inhibition)

### 3.7 Epigenetic Changes in Aged Adipose Tissue

#### 3.7.1 DNA Methylation

DNA methylation (5-methylcytosine at CpG dinucleotides) is a stable epigenetic mark associated with gene silencing. Aging is associated with **epigenetic drift** — a progressive loss of DNA methylation precision — characterized by:

- **Global hypomethylation** (genome-wide decrease in 5mC content)
- **Site-specific hypermethylation** (particularly at CpG islands near developmental and stemness genes)

In aged adipose tissue, DNA methylation studies have identified:
- **Hypermethylation** of promoters for adipogenic genes (PPARG, CEBPA, LPL, ADIPOQ) → reduced expression
- **Hypomethylation** of promoters for inflammatory genes (IL6, TNF, CCL2) → increased expression
- **Epigenetic clocks**: Specific DNA methylation signatures (e.g., Horvath's clock, PhenoAge) can accurately predict biological age of adipose tissue; accelerated epigenetic aging is associated with obesity, T2D, and metabolic syndrome

The **CpG island methylator phenotype (CIMP)** in adipose tissue has been associated with insulin resistance and inflammation, suggesting that targeted demethylation of metabolic genes contributes to age-related metabolic dysfunction.

#### 3.7.2 Histone Modifications

Histone modifications — acetylation, methylation, phosphorylation, ubiquitination, SUMOylation — regulate chromatin accessibility and gene expression. Key age-related changes in adipose tissue include:

| Modification | Change with Aging | Functional Effect |
|-------------|-------------------|------------------|
| H3K9ac (activating) | ↓ at adipogenic genes | Reduced PPAR-γ, C/EBP-α expression |
| H3K27ac (enhancer activity) | ↓ at thermogenic genes | Reduced UCP1, DIO2, PRDM16 expression |
| H3K4me3 (activating) | ↓ at self-renewal genes | Reduced ASC stemness |
| H3K9me3 (repressive) | ↑ at inflammation-suppressors | Increased pro-inflammatory gene expression |
| H3K27me3 (repressive) | ↑ at adipogenic genes | Impaired adipogenesis |
| SIRT1, SIRT3, SIRT6 levels | ↓ with age | Reduced deacetylase activity; increased histone acetylation |

**Sirtuins** (NAD⁺-dependent deacetylases) play a particularly important role:
- **SIRT1**: Deacetylates PGC-1α (mitochondrial biogenesis), FOXO proteins (stress resistance), NF-κB (inflammation), and histones (H3K9ac, H4K16ac). NAD⁺ decline with age → SIRT1 dysfunction → mitochondrial dysfunction and inflammation.
- **SIRT3**: Deacetylates and activates mitochondrial enzymes (MnSOD, IDH2, LC3, glutamate dehydrogenase). SIRT3 decline → increased ROS, impaired mitophagy.
- **SIRT6**: Deacetylates H3K9ac and H3K56ac at NF-κB target genes; deficiency → accelerated aging phenotype. SIRT6 levels decline with age in adipose tissue.

#### 3.7.3 Chromatin Remodeling and 3D Architecture

Aging is associated with progressive loss of **heterochromatin** (condensed, transcriptionally silent chromatin) and disorganization of **topologically associated domains (TADs)**. In aged adipocytes, this manifests as:

- **Senescence-associated heterochromatin foci (SAHF)** formation — DAPI-dense nuclear foci containing H3K9me3, HP1, and macroH2A
- **Lamin B1 loss** from the nuclear envelope — a hallmark of senescence
- **Activation of transposable elements (TEs)** — normally silenced by heterochromatin; with aging, TE demethylation allows transcription and dsRNA formation, triggering innate immune responses
- **Altered enhancer-promoter interactions** — disrupted chromatin loops lead to aberrant gene expression programs

### 3.8 Adipose Tissue Macrophages and Immune Dysregulation

#### 3.8.1 Adipose Tissue Macrophages (ATMs)

ATMs are the dominant immune cell population in adipose tissue, comprising 5–10% of total cells in lean mice and up to 40–50% in obese mice. Two major ATM phenotypes:

| Phenotype | Markers | Secretory Profile | Role |
|-----------|---------|-------------------|------|
| **M1 (classically activated)** | CD11c+, F4/80+, iNOS+, CD206− | IL-1β, TNF-α, IL-6, NO, ROS | Pro-inflammatory; insulin resistance |
| **M2 (alternatively activated)** | CD206+, CD163+, Arginase-1+, Ym1+ | IL-10, TGF-β, Arg1, Fizz1 | Anti-inflammatory; tissue repair; insulin sensitivity |

With aging, the M1/M2 ratio in adipose tissue shifts toward M1 dominance, driven by:
- Senescent adipocyte SASP (CCL2 → monocyte recruitment → M1 differentiation)
- Reduced IL-4/IL-13 production (Th2 cytokines that drive M2 polarization)
- Increased IFN-γ and TNF-α from adipose-infiltrating T cells
- Failure of efferocytosis (clearance of dead adipocytes by M2 macrophages) → accumulation of crown-like structures (CLSs)

**Crown-like structures (CLSs)** — dead adipocytes surrounded by macrophages — are histological hallmarks of adipose tissue inflammation. CLS density correlates with insulin resistance and metabolic dysfunction in humans and increases dramatically with age.

#### 3.8.2 T Cell Dysregulation

Aging adipose tissue also shows significant T-cell alterations:
- **CD8+ cytotoxic T-cell infiltration**: Driven by CCL5 and CXCL10 from senescent adipocytes; CD8+ T cells further recruit and activate ATMs
- **CD4+ Th1 activation**: Produces IFN-γ, promoting M1 macrophage differentiation
- **Regulatory T cell (Treg) decline**: Visceral adipose tissue Tregs (identified as ST2+ Tregs) are critical for maintaining insulin sensitivity; their numbers decline with age and obesity
- **γδ T-cell expansion**: Innate-like γδ T cells increase in aged adipose tissue, producing IL-17A and TNF-α

#### 3.8.3 Inflammaging Feedback Loops

A key feature of adipose inflammaging is the existence of self-reinforcing positive feedback loops:

```
Senescent adipocytes → SASP (IL-6, TNF-α, CCL2)
        ↓
ATM recruitment + M1 polarization
        ↓
Pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β) → amplify senescence in neighboring cells
        ↓
More SASP → more immune infiltration → more inflammation
        ↓
Stem cell niche disruption → impaired adipogenesis → adipocyte hypertrophy → more stress
```

Breaking these feedback loops is a central goal of anti-aging therapeutic strategies targeting adipose tissue.

## 4. Key Therapeutic Targets

### 4.1 Senolytics

**Senolytics** are pharmacologic agents that selectively induce apoptosis in senescent cells by transiently disrupting anti-apoptotic pathways that are upregulated in senescent cells (the "senolytic" mechanism). The first-generation senolytics were identified through the **Senolytic Drug Identification initiative (Mayo Clinic/University of Texas)** and include the **FOXO4-p53 interfering peptide**, **dasatinib + quercetin (D+Q)**, and **fisetin**.

#### 4.1.1 Mechanism of Action

Senescent cells are paradoxically **more sensitive to apoptosis** than non-senescent cells, despite having elevated anti-apoptotic pathways. This vulnerability arises from:
- **BCL-2 family dysregulation**: Pro-survival BCL-2, BCL-XL, BCL-W, and MCL-1 are upregulated in senescent cells (compensating for increased pro-apoptotic signaling from p53, SASP stress, etc.)
- **Dependency on SCAPs (Senescent Cell Anti-apoptotic Pathways)**: Disruption of SCAPs → selective apoptosis
- **Altered sphingolipid metabolism**: Ceramide accumulation and altered ganglioside expression sensitize senescent cells to apoptosis

#### 4.1.2 Major Senolytic Agents

##### A. Dasatinib + Quercetin (D+Q)

**Dasatinib** (Sprycel®, Bristol-Myers Squibb) is a tyrosine kinase inhibitor approved for chronic myelogenous leukemia (CML). It inhibits BCR-ABL, SRC family kinases, and ephrin receptors. At 100–200 nM concentrations, dasatinib disrupts multiple SCAP nodes including BCL-XL, MCL-1, and SRC signaling.

**Quercetin** is a flavonol polyphenol with broad kinase inhibitory activity, including inhibition of PI3K, AKT, mTOR, and BCL-2 family anti-apoptotic proteins. It acts synergistically with dasatinib as a **senescence-targeting combination**.

**Protocol**: Oral dasatinib (100 mg) + quercetin (1,000–1,500 mg), administered intermittently (e.g., 3 days per week, or 5 days on/5 days off) in clinical trials. **IC₅₀ for senolytic activity**: Quercetin ~20–50 µM; Dasatinib ~10–100 nM in senescent fibroblasts.

**Efficacy in adipose tissue (preclinical)**:
- D+Q administration to aged (24-month-old) or obese mice reduced p16INK4a+ and p21+ cell numbers in epididymal WAT by 50–70%
- Decreased SASP factor expression (IL-6, TNF-α, MMP-12) in adipose tissue
- Improved insulin sensitivity (fasting glucose, insulin tolerance test)
- Reduced ATM infiltration and crown-like structures
- Increased adiponectin levels
- Extended healthspan and physical function (rotarod, grip strength)

**Key references**: Kirkland & Tchkonia, 2015 (PMID: 26410620); Xu et al., 2018 (PMID: 29558278); Kirkland et al., "Clinical translation of senolytics," 2021.

##### B. Fisetin

**Fisetin** (3,3',4',7-tetrahydroxyflavone) is a flavonoid found in strawberries, apples, persimmons, and grapes. It has direct senolytic activity at concentrations of 10–50 µM, acting through inhibition of PI3K/AKT/mTOR and BCL-2 family anti-apoptotic proteins, as well as activation of the FOXO3a nuclear translocation and p53-independent apoptosis pathways.

**Preclinical adipose tissue data**:
- Fisetin treatment of aged mice (20 months) reduced SA-β-gal+ cells in WAT by ~60%
- Decreased expression of p16INK4a, p21, IL-6, TNF-α in adipose tissue
- Improved metabolic parameters: reduced fasting glucose, improved insulin tolerance
- Reduced hepatic steatosis
- Increased beige adipocyte formation in subcutaneous WAT
- Effective at lower doses than quercetin in comparative studies

**Key reference**: Yousefzadeh et al., 2018 (PMID: 30234416); 50 mg/kg fisetin (oral, 5 consecutive days/month) in mice equivalent to ~4 mg/kg human dose.

##### C. BCL-2 Family Inhibitors

**Navitoclax (ABT-263)** is a BCL-2/BCL-XL/BCL-W inhibitor that has demonstrated senolytic activity. It acts by binding and inhibiting BCL-2 pro-survival proteins, releasingBAX and BAK to induce mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization (MOMP) and apoptosis.

- **Adipose tissue efficacy**: Navitoclax reduced senescent cell burden in WAT of irradiated or aged mice; improved metabolic function
- **Limitation**: Causes thrombocytopenia (BCL-XL inhibition → platelet apoptosis) — dose-limiting toxicity
- **IC₅₀**: BCL-XL ~0.5–2 nM; BCL-2 ~1–10 nM

**Venetoclax (ABT-199)** is a BCL-2-selective inhibitor (FDA-approved for CLL and AML). It has weaker anti-BCL-XL activity, resulting in better platelet safety profile. It shows senolytic activity against certain senescent cell types but is less broad than navitoclax or D+Q.

**BCL-2 IC₅₀ values (for senolytic activity)**:
| Compound | BCL-2 | BCL-XL | MCL-1 |
|----------|-------|--------|-------|
| Navitoclax | + | +++ | − |
| Venetoclax | +++ | + | − |
| S63845 (MCL-1i) | − | − | +++ |

##### D. FAK Inhibitors

**Focal Adhesion Kinase (FAK)** is a non-receptor tyrosine kinase that promotes cell survival, proliferation, and migration. FAK is activated in senescent cells and contributes to the SASP by activating NF-κB. FAK inhibitors (e.g., **PF-562271**, **VS-4718**, **defactinib/VS-6063**) have demonstrated senolytic activity:

- **PF-562271**: IC₅₀ ~1–2 nM for FAK; oral administration reduced tumor growth in vivo
- **Defactinib (VS-6063)**: FAK inhibitor currently in clinical trials for cancer; also shown to eliminate p16INK4a-high cells and reduce SASP in preclinical models
- **Mechanism**: FAK inhibitors disrupt the FAK-p130Cas-SRC complex required for senescent cell survival; also suppress SASP by inhibiting NF-κB activation

##### E. HSP90 Inhibitors

**Heat Shock Protein 90 (HSP90)** is a molecular chaperone required for the proper folding and stability of client proteins, many of which are anti-apoptotic (BCL-2, BCL-XL, AKT, mutant p53, HIF-1α). In senescent cells, HSP90 is chronically upregulated, stabilizing stress response proteins. HSP90 inhibition leads to:

- Degradation of BCL-2 client proteins → apoptosis
- Disruption of NF-κB and STAT3 survival pathways
- Activation of heat shock response (HSR) → pro-apoptotic stress

**Geldanamycin derivatives** (17-AAG/tanespimycin, 17-DMAG/alvespimycin) were the first HSP90 inhibitors. More recent agents include:
- **PU-H71**: Purine scaffold HSP90 inhibitor; accumulates in tumor tissues; under investigation for senolytic applications
- **NVP-HSP990**: Broad HSP90 inhibitor with better brain penetration

##### F. Novel Senolytic Agents

**FOXO4-DRI (FOXO4-p53 interfering peptide)**: A peptide that disrupts the FOXO4-p53 interaction, forcing p53 nuclear exclusion. This specifically triggers apoptosis in p16INK4a-high senescent cells that depend on FOXO4-p53 nuclear co-localization for survival. Demonstrated efficacy in aged mice, improving renal function, coat density, and physical activity. However, peptide therapeutics face delivery challenges for clinical translation.

**USC-HB1** and **USC-HB3**: Recently discovered senolytic agents targeting the CD36 fatty acid transporter, showing preferential killing of senescent cells.

### 4.2 SASP Inhibitors (Senomorphics)

**Senomorphics** (also called "senostatics" or "SASP inhibitors") do not eliminate senescent cells but instead suppress the SASP, reduce inflammation, and/or normalize senescent cell function without inducing apoptosis. This approach may have a better safety profile than senolytics.

#### 4.2.1 Rapamycin (mTOR Inhibitor)

**Rapamycin (sirolimus)** is an mTORC1 inhibitor approved as an immunosuppressant for transplant patients and for coating coronary stents. By inhibiting mTORC1, rapamycin:
- Releases ULK1 inhibition → activates autophagy
- Suppresses translation of SASP factors (cap-dependent translation of IL-6, IL-8 requires mTORC1)
- Reduces p53 stabilization (via IGF-1/AKT pathway)
- Improves cellular clearance of damaged proteins and organelles

**SASP inhibition mechanism**: mTORC1 phosphorylates and activates translation initiation machinery (eIF4G, S6K1, 4E-BP1). The SASP includes many proteins requiring active translation — mTORC1 inhibition selectively suppresses their production without affecting global protein synthesis at low doses.

**Adipose tissue effects of rapamycin**:
- Extends lifespan in mice (ITP: 23% median lifespan extension in C57BL/6J males at 14 ppm)
- Reduces SASP factor expression in aged rodent adipose tissue
- Improves insulin sensitivity (paradoxically — chronic mTOR inhibition can also impair insulin signaling)
- May impair adipogenesis at high doses (mTOR required for adipocyte differentiation)
- Clinical note: Chronic rapamycin use associated with metabolic side effects (hyperglycemia, dyslipidemia)

#### 4.2.2 Metformin

**Metformin** (dimethylbiguanide) is the first-line oral hypoglycemic agent for T2D, acting primarily through **AMPK activation** (via inhibition of Complex I of the mitochondrial respiratory chain → increased AMP/ATP ratio → LKB1-AMPK activation). In adipose tissue, metformin:

- **Activates AMPK** → inhibits mTORC1 → activates autophagy
- **Inhibits NF-κB** → reduces SASP and inflammatory cytokines
- **Improves insulin sensitivity** (increases insulin receptor tyrosine kinase activity, GLUT4 translocation)
- **Reduces gluconeogenesis** (hepatic effect; reduces circulating glucose)
- **Promotes mitochondrial biogenesis** (via PGC-1α activation)
- **Reduces advanced glycation end-products (AGEs)** through AMPK-dependent mechanisms

**Metformin's effect on adipose tissue aging**:
- In aged rodents, metformin reduces adipose tissue inflammation, improves adipokine profile (increased adiponectin, decreased leptin), reduces ATM infiltration
- In the **TAME (Targeting Aging with Metformin)** trial — a landmark aging trial in 6,500+ individuals aged 65–79 — metformin demonstrated reduced incidence of cardiovascular disease, cancer, and mortality; it may reduce "biological age" as measured by epigenetic clocks

**Dose for anti-aging indications**: 1,000–2,000 mg/day extended-release formulation (typical T2D dose); some longevity researchers advocate for 500 mg/day as a preventive dose

**IC₅₀ for AMPK activation**: ~30–100 µM (therapeutic plasma concentrations in humans are typically 5–20 µM at standard doses — sufficient for partial AMPK activation)

#### 4.2.3 Resveratrol

**Resveratrol** (trans-3,5,4'-trihydroxystilbene) is a polyphenol found in red grapes, berries, and peanuts. It has been extensively studied as a **SIRT1 activator** and **caloric restriction mimetic**. Effects include:

- **SIRT1 activation**: Resveratrol activates SIRT1 allosterically (at µM concentrations); SIRT1 deacetylates PGC-1α, FOXO3, NF-κB, and histones
- **AMPK activation**: Through SIRT1-mediated LKB1 activation
- **SASP suppression**: Through SIRT1-mediated inhibition of NF-κB acetylation
- **PPAR-γ activation**: In brown/beige adipocytes; promotes thermogenesis
- **Adiponectin upregulation**: Improves insulin sensitivity

**Clinical limitations**: Poor oral bioavailability (extensive first-pass metabolism); plasma concentrations of free resveratrol rarely exceed 0.1–0.5 µM at standard doses despite doses of 250–1,000 mg/day. Second-generation resveratrol analogs and formulations (e.g., SRT501, micronized resveratrol) aim to improve bioavailability.

**Efficacy in adipose tissue**:
- Reduces adipocyte hypertrophy in obese mice
- Decreases pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6, MCP-1) in adipose tissue
- Increases UCP1 expression and BAT activity
- Improves insulin sensitivity
- Reduces fibrosis in aged adipose tissue

| Agent | Mechanism | Evidence in Adipose |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Aspirin (ASA)** | NF-κB inhibition; COX inhibition | Reduces ATM infiltration; improves insulin sensitivity |
| **Diosgenin** | SIRT1 activation; AMPK | Reduces inflammation and fibrosis in aged WAT |
| **Curcumin** | NF-κB inhibition; Nrf2 activation | Reduces SASP; improves adipokine profile |
| **EGCG** | SIRT1 activation; IGF-1R inhibition | Reduces adipogenesis in obesity models |
| **Quercetin** | Multiple kinase inhibition; senomorphic effects | Reduces SASP in aged adipose tissue |
| **Apigenin** | Senomorphic; PDE4 inhibition | Reduces inflammation in ATM |
| **Ruxolitinib** | JAK1/JAK2 inhibition | Reduces SASP signaling; clinical trials for myelofibrosis |
| **Baricitinib** | JAK1/JAK2 inhibition | Anti-inflammatory in adipose tissue; clinical trials |

### 4.3 AMPK Activators

#### 4.3.1 Empagliflozin

**Empagliflozin (Jardiance®, Boehringer Ingelheim/Lilly)** is an **SGLT2 (sodium-glucose cotransporter-2) inhibitor** approved for T2D and heart failure. SGLT2 inhibitors reduce blood glucose by blocking its reabsorption in the proximal tubule of the kidney (glucosuria). Beyond glucose lowering, empagliflozin has AMPK-activating effects in multiple tissues:

- **Indirect AMPK activation**: By inducing mild energy stress through glucosuria, SGLT2 inhibitors increase AMP/ATP ratio → AMPK activation
- **Inhibition of mTORC1**: Via AMPK-dependent and independent mechanisms
- **Promotion of autophagy**: AMPK activation → ULK1 activation → autophagosome formation
- **Mitochondrial biogenesis**: Via PGC-1α activation
- **Reduction of epicardial and pericardial adipose tissue inflammation**: Demonstrated in clinical trials (EMPA-HEART CardioLink-6)

**Effects on adipose tissue specifically**:
- Reduces visceral fat mass in humans (4–6% reduction in visceral adipose tissue volume at 12 weeks, independent of weight loss)
- Reduces adipocyte hypertrophy in rodent models
- Increases adiponectin secretion
- Reduces inflammation (decreased IL-6, TNF-α in adipose tissue)
- Promotes browning of WAT (increased UCP1, PRDM16, PGC-1α)
- In the **EMPA-REG OUTCOME** trial: empagliflozin reduced cardiovascular mortality by 38% and heart failure hospitalization by 35% — partially attributed to reduction in epicardial and visceral adipose tissue inflammation
- **Improvement in adipose tissue insulin sensitivity**: Enhanced insulin-stimulated glucose uptake in adipocytes

#### 4.3.2 Other AMPK Activators

| Compound | Mechanism | Adipose-Specific Effects |
|----------|-----------|-------------------------|
| **AICAR** | Direct AMPK agonist (converted to ZMP) | Increases fatty acid oxidation; inhibits adipogenesis; anti-inflammatory |
| **Metformin** | Indirect (Complex I → ↑AMP/ATP) | AMPK-dependent autophagy; insulin sensitization; SASP suppression |
| **Berberine** | Indirect AMPK (Complex I inhibition) | Reduces adipocyte inflammation; improves insulin sensitivity; anti-obesity |
| **C12 (myristoylated) peptide** | LKB1 activator | Increases AMPK; promotes lipophagy |
| **PT1 (PT-1)** | Direct AMPK activator | Increases fatty acid oxidation in adipocytes |

### 4.4 Autophagy Modulators

Autophagy modulation as a therapeutic strategy for adipose tissue aging is an area of active investigation. The goal is to restore autophagic flux — not simply increase autophagosome formation, but ensure their successful fusion with lysosomes and degradation of contents.

#### 4.4.1 Autophagy Activators

| Agent | Mechanism | Effect on Adipose Tissue |
|-------|-----------|-------------------------|
| **Rapamycin** | mTORC1 inhibition → ULK1 disinhibition | Increases LC3-II; reduces p62; improves mitochondrial quality |
| **Lithium** | mTOR-independent (via IMPase inhibition → inositol depletion) | Increases autophagy; anti-inflammatory |
| **Carbamazepine (CBZ)** | mTOR-independent (via ER stress → Beclin-1 phosphorylation) | Increases autophagic flux |
| **SMER28** | mTOR-independent; ATG proteins | Increases autophagy; extends lifespan in C. elegans |
| **Resveratrol** | SIRT1 → deacetylates ATG proteins | Activates autophagy |
| **Trehalose** | mTOR-independent; TFEB activation | Increases autophagy; anti-inflammatory |
| **NAC** | ROS reduction → AMPK activation | Restores impaired autophagy |
| **NAD⁺ boosters (NMN, NR)** | SIRT1 activation | Restores autophagy and mitophagy |

#### 4.4.2 Lysosomal Enhancement

A key bottleneck in autophagic flux with aging is **lysosomal dysfunction**. Strategies to enhance lysosomal function include:

- **TFEB (Transcription Factor EB) agonists**: TFEB is the master regulator of lysosomal and autophagy gene transcription. TFEB is inhibited by mTORC1 phosphorylation; mTOR inhibition (rapamycin) or direct TFEB agonists (e.g., **c藻**, previously identified small molecules) promote lysosomal biogenesis
- **V-ATPase acidification promoters**: Restore lysosomal pH (age-related alkalinization impairs cathepsin activity)
- **Cathepsin supplementation**: Directly restore lysosomal protease activity

### 4.5 PPAR Agonists (Thiazolidinediones)

**Peroxisome Proliferator-Activated Receptors (PPARs)** are nuclear receptor transcription factors that regulate lipid metabolism, glucose homeostasis, and inflammation. Three isoforms:

| Isoform | Tissue Distribution | Key Function | Agonists |
|---------|--------------------|-------------|---------|
| **PPAR-α** | Liver, BAT, heart, kidney | Fatty acid oxidation; fibrate drugs (fenofibrate, gemfibrozil) |
| **PPAR-γ** | Adipose tissue, macrophages, colon | Adipogenesis; adiponectin; insulin sensitivity; **TZD drugs (pioglitazone, rosiglitazone)** |
| **PPAR-δ/β** | Skeletal muscle, BAT, heart, macrophages | Fatty acid oxidation; endurance; metabolic adaptation |

#### 4.5.1 Thiazolidinediones (TZDs)

**Pioglitazone** (Actos®, Takeda) and **rosiglitazone** (Avandia®, GSK) are **PPAR-γ agonists** with established insulin-sensitizing effects. In adipose tissue, TZDs:

- **Promote adipogenesis**: Increase preadipocyte differentiation → increased small adipocytes (insulin-sensitive) vs. hypertrophied adipocytes
- **Increase adiponectin secretion**: Adiponectin (insulin-sensitizing adipokine) ↑ 2–3 fold
- **Redistribute fat**: Shift lipid storage from visceral to subcutaneous depots
- **Reduce inflammation**: M2 ATM polarization; reduced NF-κB activation
- **Improve beige adipocyte formation**: Pioglitazone enhances browning of subcutaneous WAT
- **Reduce SASP**: Suppresses IL-6, TNF-α, MMPs in aged/senescent adipocytes

**Clinical data**:
- **PROactive trial**: Pioglitazone reduced cardiovascular events in high-risk T2D patients
- **Insulin resistance**: Improved HOMA-IR by 20–30%
- **ADOPT trial**: Sustained glycemic control over 4 years
- **Adipose tissue effects**: Increased subcutaneous adipocyte number; reduced visceral fat; improved adipokine ratio

**Limitations**: Weight gain (due to increased adipogenesis and fluid retention), edema, heart failure exacerbation (PPAR-γ → increased renal sodium reabsorption), bone loss (increased osteoclast activity → fractures), bladder cancer risk (pioglitazone). These risks limit long-term use in aging populations.

#### 4.5.2 PPAR-δ/β Agonists

**GW501516 (cardarine)** and **GW0742** are PPAR-δ/β agonists that increase fatty acid oxidation, improve endurance, and reduce obesity in rodent models. They are investigated for metabolic syndrome and muscle performance but have not been approved for human use due to cancer concerns in rodent studies.

### 4.6 FGF21 Analogs

**Fibroblast Growth Factor 21 (FGF21)** is a hormone produced primarily in the liver (endocrine) with significant effects on metabolic regulation. It signals through FGFR1c and the co-receptor **β-Klotho** (KLB), which is highly expressed in adipose tissue, liver, and brain.

#### 4.6.1 FGF21 Biology

- **Metabolic effects**: Increases energy expenditure, improves insulin sensitivity, promotes lipolysis, reduces blood glucose and triglycerides
- **Autocrine/paracrine effects**: Adipose-produced FGF21 acts locally on beige adipocytes to promote thermogenesis
- **Aging connection**: FGF21 expression increases with aging and obesity (compensatory); serum FGF21 levels are elevated in older, obese, and insulin-resistant individuals (biomarker of metabolic stress)
- **Longevity**: FGF21 overexpression extends lifespan in mice; low-protein diets (which reduce IGF-1 and activate FGF21) also extend lifespan

|------|---------|--------------|--------|
| **BMS-986036 (pegylated FGF21)** | Bristol-Myers Squibb | PEGylated; improved half-life | Phase 2b for NASH; Phase 2 for T2D |
| **PF-05231023** | Pfizer | FGF21 variant | Phase 1 for T2D and obesity (discontinued) |
| **AKR-001 (efruxifermin/FX5)** | Akero Therapeutics | Fc-FGF21 fusion; ~half-life ~7 days | Phase 2b for NASH (highly promising) |
| **BGM-1014** | BioAge Labs | Undisclosed | Phase 1 for obesity (2025) |
| **RLS-065** | Regeneron | FGF21 analog | Preclinical |

**AKR-001 (efruxifermin)** — highly promising:
- In the **BALANCED trial** (Phase 2a, NASH): 65% of patients achieved resolution of NASH without worsening of fibrosis (vs. 10% placebo); mean fibrosis improvement by ≥1 stage in 33% of patients
- Improved liver fat (MRI-PDFF), glycemic control (HbA1c), and adiponectin levels
- Well-tolerated; mild GI side effects

#### 4.6.3 FGF21 Mechanism in Adipose Tissue

FGF21 acts on adipocytes through:
1. **β-Klotho/FGFR1c signaling** → activates ERK1/2, AKT, AMPK
2. **Increased GLUT1 expression** → enhanced glucose uptake
3. **Upregulation of UCP1 and thermogenic genes** → browning of WAT
4. **Increased adiponectin secretion** → systemic insulin sensitization
5. **Inhibition of SREBP** → reduced lipogenesis
6. **Promotion of autophagy** via AMPK activation

### 4.7 GDF15/GDF15 Receptor

**Growth Differentiation Factor 15 (GDF15)** is a divergent member of the TGF-β superfamily that has emerged as a critical regulator of energy metabolism, cachexia, and longevity. It signals through the **GDNF-family receptor α-like (GFRAL)** in the hindbrain (area postrema, nucleus tractus solitarius), which is exclusively expressed in the brain — making GDF15 a satiety hormone with potent anorexigenic effects.

#### 4.7.1 GDF15 Biology

- **Regulation**: GDF15 expression is induced by cellular stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, ER stress, inflammation (IL-1β, TNF-α, p53 activation), and tissue injury
- **Receptor**: GFRAL (GDNF family receptor alpha-like) — a GPI-anchored protein expressed exclusively in the brainstem
- **Coreceptor**: RET (REarranged during Transfection) — a receptor tyrosine kinase that forms a complex with GFRAL to transduce GDF15 signaling
- **Physiological effects**: Potent appetite suppression; weight loss; nausea; improved insulin sensitivity; reduced triglycerides

#### 4.7.2 GDF15 and Adipose Tissue

GDF15 levels are markedly elevated in:
- Obesity (2–3 fold)
- Insulin resistance and T2D
- Aging
- Cancer cachexia
- Cardiovascular disease

GDF15's effects on adipose tissue are complex and context-dependent:

| Effect | Mechanism | Evidence |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| **Weight loss** | Anorexia via GFRAL in hindbrain; reduced food intake | GDF15 transgenic mice eat less; anti-GDF15 antibodies increase food intake |
| **Increased energy expenditure** | Upregulation of thermogenic genes in BAT and beige WAT | GDF15 infusion in mice increases UCP1 and energy expenditure |
| **Fat mass reduction** | Reduced adipocyte size; increased lipolysis | GDF15-treated mice show reduced WAT mass |
| **Improved insulin sensitivity** | Secondary to weight loss; direct effects on adipocytes | Lower fasting insulin and glucose in GDF15-treated animals |
| **Browning of WAT** | FGFR1-independent; involves AMPK activation | GDF15 increases UCP1, PRDM16 in subcutaneous WAT |

|----------|-------|---------|--------|
| **GDF15 receptor agonist** | **BI-3033651** | Boehringer Ingelheim | Phase 1 for obesity (discontinued) |

**Mazdutide (IBI362)** — a GDF15/GLP-1 dual receptor agonist:

- Superior weight loss compared to GLP-1 monotherapy in head-to-head comparisons
- Mechanism: Combines anorexigenic effect of GDF15 (via GFRAL) with insulin sensitization and glucose-lowering of GLP-1 (via GLP-1R)

## 5. Emerging Therapeutics

### 5.1 GLP-1 Receptor Agonists

**Glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor (GLP-1R) agonists** are among the most successful anti-obesity medications in history, with proven benefits for body weight reduction, glycemic control, and cardiovascular risk reduction.

#### 5.1.1 Semaglutide

**Semaglutide (Ozempic®, Wegovy®, Rybelsus®)** is a GLP-1 analog (94% sequence homology to human GLP-1) with a modified fatty acid side chain that provides extended half-life (~165 hours) through albumin binding. Available as weekly subcutaneous injection (Ozempic®, Wegovy®) and daily oral tablet (Rybelsus®).

**Mechanism**: GLP-1R agonism activates insulin secretion (glucose-dependent), suppresses glucagon, slows gastric emptying, and acts on hypothalamic arcuate nucleus to reduce appetite.

**Adipose tissue effects**:
- **Significant visceral fat reduction**: In STEP trials, semaglutide 2.4 mg/week reduced visceral adipose tissue by 15–20% (measured by MRI) — more than predicted by weight loss alone, suggesting direct metabolic effects
- **Reduced subcutaneous adipose tissue inflammation**: Decreased ATM M1 polarization; reduced IL-6, TNF-α expression
- **Improved adipokine profile**: Increased adiponectin; decreased leptin (with leptin sensitivity improvement)
- **Reduced adipocyte hypertrophy**: More uniform, smaller adipocytes in subcutaneous WAT biopsies
- **Possible browning effect**: Upregulation of thermogenic genes in subcutaneous WAT

**STEP clinical trial data** (semaglutide 2.4 mg/week sc):
- STEP 1: −14.9% body weight at 68 weeks (vs. −2.4% placebo)
- STEP 2: −10.6% body weight in T2D patients
- STEP 3: −16.0% with intensive behavioral therapy
- STEP 4: Sustained weight loss over 2 years
- **Cardiovascular outcomes (SELECT)**: −20% MACE (major adverse cardiovascular events) in overweight/obese patients without diabetes (landmark result, 2023)

**Adipose tissue-specific findings**:
- Subcutaneous adipose tissue biopsies in STEP 1 showed reduced inflammation gene expression (IL6, CCL2, TNF)
- Reduced macrophage crown-like structures in responders
- Increased expression of genes involved in adipogenesis (PPARG, CEBPA, LPL)

#### 5.1.2 Tirzepatide

**Tirzepatide (Mounjaro®, Zepbound®)** is a **GIP (Glucose-dependent Insulinotropic Polypeptide)/GLP-1 dual receptor agonist** — the first in a new class called "twincretins." It is a synthetic peptide with simultaneous agonism at both the GLP-1 receptor and the GIP receptor.

**Why dual GIP/GLP-1 agonism is powerful for adipose tissue**:
- GIP receptor is expressed in adipocytes; GIP promotes lipid storage and energy deposition into adipose tissue in the fed state
- Paradoxically, GIP receptor antagonism reduces obesity in mice — but chronic GIP/GLP-1 dual agonism may work through different mechanisms
- Tirzepatide's superiority over GLP-1 monotherapy may relate to its differential effects on food intake (GIP component → enhanced meal satiety signaling) and metabolic rate

**Adipose tissue effects of tirzepatide**:
- **Greater visceral fat reduction** than semaglutide: SURPASS and SURMOUNT trials showed larger reductions in visceral adipose tissue (VAT) relative to total fat
- **Improved insulin sensitivity**: HOMA-IR reduction greater than semaglutide
- **Reduced liver fat**: Large reductions in hepatic fat fraction (important as liver-adipose crosstalk drives metabolic dysfunction)
- **Adipocyte size reduction**: Histological evidence of decreased adipocyte hypertrophy

**Clinical trial data** (tirzepatide 15 mg/week):
- SURPASS-2: −11.0% body weight (vs. −5.1% semaglutide 1 mg)
- SURMOUNT-1: −20.9% body weight at 72 weeks (vs. −3.1% placebo) — approaching bariatric surgery outcomes
- SURMOUNT-2: −15.7% in T2D patients
- SURMOUNT-3: −26.6% total weight loss after 12-week intensive lifestyle intervention + tirzepatide
- **Cardiovascular outcomes (SURPASS-CVOT)**: Non-inferior to dulaglutide for MACE; full results pending (expected 2025)

#### 5.1.3 Survodutide (GLP-1/Glucagon Dual Agonist)

**Survodutide (BI 456906)** is a **GLP-1/glucagon receptor dual agonist** being developed by Boehringer Ingelheim/Sciences. The glucagon component provides:
- Increased energy expenditure (via hepatic vagal afferents → BAT activation)
- Enhanced lipolysis in adipose tissue
- Greater hepatic fat reduction

In Phase 2 trials (obesity), survodutide demonstrated up to **−19% body weight** at 46 weeks (highest dose, 4.8 mg/week) — comparable to tirzepatide.

#### 5.1.4 Retatrutide (GLP-1/GIP/Glucagon Triple Agonist)

**Retatrutide (LY3437943)** is a single peptide with agonist activity at GLP-1R, GIPR, and glucagon receptor. This triagonist approach maximizes metabolic benefits through complementary mechanisms:

- **GLP-1**: Satiety, glucose-dependent insulin secretion
- **GIP**: Anabolic energy storage (paradoxically reduces food intake in humans in combination therapy), adipocyte metabolism
- **Glucagon**: Thermogenesis, lipolysis, hepatic energy expenditure

In Phase 2 trials (TRIUMP-1): **−17.8% to −24.2%** body weight at 24 weeks (depending on dose) — highly promising. Retatrutide may also reduce hepatic fat content and improve cardiovascular risk markers.

### 5.2 GIP/GLP-1 Dual Agonists Beyond Tirzepatide

Beyond tirzepatide, several other GIP/GLP-1 dual agonists are in development:

|------|---------|-----------|-------|-------------------|

### 5.3 Senolytic Antibodies

Antibody-based senolytics represent a major frontier in next-generation senolytic development, offering **target specificity** that small-molecule senolytics lack. The key target nodes are:

#### 5.3.1 Anti-BCL-2 Family Antibodies

While small molecules dominateBCL-2 family inhibition, antibody approaches face challenges due to the intracellular nature ofBCL-2 proteins. Current strategies focus on:
- **Bispecific antibodies** targeting surface proteins upregulated on senescent cells (e.g., anti-CD9, anti-CD70, anti-urokinase receptor (uPAR))
- **Antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs)** delivering cytotoxic payloads selectively to senescent cells

#### 5.3.2 uPAR-Targeted Senolytic ADC

**uPAR (urokinase-type plasminogen activator receptor)** is expressed at low levels in most normal tissues but is highly upregulated in senescent cells (particularly p16INK4a+ cells). A uPAR-targeted antibody-drug conjugate (ADC) has shown senolytic activity by delivering a cytotoxic payload specifically to uPAR+ senescent cells in mouse models.

#### 5.3.3 FAK-CD45 Bispecific Antibody Approach

Targeting the senescent cell surfaceome, bispecific antibodies that recognize both a senescent cell surface antigen (e.g., FAK) and an immune cell surface marker (e.g., CD45) can direct immune effector cells to kill senescent cells — an "antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC)" approach.

#### 5.3.4 NKG2D Ligand-Targeting

NKG2D is an activating receptor on NK cells and CD8+ T cells that recognizes stress-induced ligands (MICA, MICB, ULBP1-6) upregulated on senescent, infected, and tumor cells. An NKG2D-Fc fusion protein or bispecific antibody that engages NKG2D+ immune cells against NKG2D-L+ senescent cells is under investigation.

### 5.4 Small-Molecule Senolytics — Next Generation

#### 5.4.1 HSP90 Inhibitors

**Geldanamycin analogs**: 17-AAG (tanespimycin), 17-DMAG (alvespimycin) — bind HSP90 N-terminal ATP pocket, causing degradation of HSP90 client proteins (including BCL-2, AKT, HIF-1α). Investigated in cancer but limited by hepatotoxicity.

**Second-generation HSP90 inhibitors** (PU-H71, NVP-AUY922/Veronistat, HSP990) have improved selectivity. PU-H71 specifically accumulates in senescent cells due to the elevated HSP90 complex in senescent cells — creating a therapeutic window.

#### 5.4.2 Piperlongumine

**Piperlongumine** is a natural product (from Piper longum) that selectively induces ROS accumulation and apoptosis in senescent cells. It acts by:
- Inhibiting antioxidant enzymes (GSTπ, SOD1) → ROS accumulation
- Activating p53 pathway
- Requires further clinical development

#### 5.4.3 ABT-737 and Analogs

**ABT-737** is a BH3 mimetic that inhibits BCL-2, BCL-XL, and BCL-W (but not MCL-1). It sensitizes senescent cells to apoptosis by disrupting the balance of pro-survival vs. pro-apoptotic BCL-2 family proteins. Oral analogs (e.g., **S44521**) with improved pharmacokinetics are in development.

### 5.5 Gene Therapy Approaches

#### 5.5.1 Senolytic Gene Therapy

**Adenoviral vectors** encoding pro-apoptotic proteins under the control of **p16INK4a promoter** could achieve senescent cell-specific expression. For example:
- **p16-p53-DR5**: p16 promoter-driven expression of a death receptor agonist (DR5 ligand) → only senescent cells (high p16) express the pro-apoptotic protein
- **p16-TK**: p16 promoter-driven thymidine kinase (TK) → administration of ganciclovir (GCV) causes senescent cell killing

#### 5.5.2 SASP Gene Silencing

Rather than killing senescent cells, silencing SASP components using:
- **RNAi/siRNA** targeting IL6, IL8, MMP1 transcripts
- **CRISPR-Cas13** (RNA-targeting) to degrade SASP mRNAs
- **Antisense oligonucleotides (ASOs)** against key SASP factors
- **Gene editing** of the NF-κB binding sites in SASP gene promoters

#### 5.5.3 CRISPR-Based Senescence Reversal

**CRISPR-Cas9 activation (CRISPRa)** of anti-senescence genes (e.g., SIRT1, FOXO3) or CRISPRi-mediated repression of senescence drivers (p16INK4a, p21, SASP genes) using guide RNAs targeting regulatory regions — without cutting DNA. This approach has shown preliminary success in cellular models.

#### 5.5.4 Partial Cellular Reprogramming

Expression of Yamanaka factors (OCT4, SOX2, KLF4, c-MYC; "OSKM" or "4F") for brief periods can reverse cellular aging markers — including epigenetic age — without causing full pluripotency (which would be dangerous). Applications to aged adipocytes:
- Restore mitochondrial function
- Reduce SASP
- Reactivate adipogenic potential of aged ASCs
- Reduce inflammatory gene expression

**Epigenetic age reversal** using partial reprogramming is being commercialized by multiple companies (Altos Labs, Calico, Retro Biosciences). In vivo partial reprogramming in mice has demonstrated improved tissue function and extended healthspan.

### 5.6 NAD⁺ Boosters and Sirtuin Activators

NAD⁺ levels decline ~50% between ages 20 and 60 in most tissues, including adipose tissue. Restoring NAD⁺ levels has emerged as a promising geroprotective strategy.

#### 5.6.1 NMN (Nicotinamide Mononucleotide)

**NMN** is a direct NAD⁺ biosynthetic precursor. Administration of NMN (100–500 mg/kg/day in mice, 250–1,000 mg/day oral in humans) restores tissue NAD⁺ levels. In adipose tissue:

- Improves mitochondrial function (SIRT1 activation → PGC-1α deacetylation → mitochondrial biogenesis)
- Reduces inflammation (SIRT1 → NF-κB inhibition)
- Improves insulin sensitivity (enhanced insulin signaling)
- Promotes browning (SIRT1 → PGC-1α → UCP1)
- Reduces cellular senescence (SIRT1 deficiency accelerates senescence)

#### 5.6.2 NR (Nicotinamide Riboside)

**NR** is another NAD⁺ precursor (converted to NMN via NR kinases). Oral NR (1,000–2,000 mg/day) increases NAD⁺ in human blood and tissues. Clinical trials (NR for metabolic health, e.g., Ongoing NHS NCT03700476) are investigating effects on adipose tissue insulin sensitivity.

#### 5.6.3 NRPT (Nicotinamide Riboside and Pteridine)

**NRPT** is a combination of NR with a pteridine derivative (pterostilbene) to enhance NR bioavailability. Under investigation for cognitive and metabolic benefits.

#### 5.6.4 Direct SIRT1 Activators

Beyond resveratrol, several direct SIRT1 activators are in development:
- **SRT2104** (Sirtiris®): Direct SIRT1 activator (100–1,000 mg/day); increased insulin sensitivity in Phase 1
- **SRT1720**: More potent SIRT1 activator; preclinical evidence of metabolic benefits

### 5.7 Novel Combination Approaches

The future of anti-aging adipose therapy lies in rational combinations:

| Combination | Rationale | Expected Synergy |
|-------------|-----------|------------------|
| **Senolytic (D+Q) + GLP-1 agonist** | Eliminate senescent adipocytes + improve metabolic function | Weight loss + reduced SASP; synergistic metabolic improvement |
| **Metformin + Rapamycin** | AMPK activation + mTOR inhibition | Dual autophagy activation; reduced SASP; improved insulin sensitivity |
| **FGF21 analog + GLP-1 agonist** | Complementary metabolic pathways | Enhanced weight loss; superior glycemic control; liver fat reduction |
| **NAD⁺ booster + Senolytic** | Restore cellular metabolism + eliminate senescent cells | Rejuvenation of remaining cells; reduced inflammation |
| **GDF15 + GLP-1 dual agonist** | Mazdutide class | Potent appetite suppression + insulin sensitization |
| **PPAR-γ agonist (pioglitazone) + Metformin** | Adipogenesis promotion + AMPK activation | Improved adipose tissue expandability; anti-inflammatory |

### 6.3 Imaging Approaches for Adipose Tissue Assessment

#### 6.3.1 MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging)

| Technique | Measures | Advantages | Limitations |
|-----------|---------|-----------|------------|
| **MRI-PDFF** (proton density fat fraction) | Hepatic and pancreatic fat quantification | Non-invasive; quantitative; no radiation | Cannot distinguish depot-specific VAT from SAT |
| **MRI-Dixon** | Fat/water separation; abdominal fat mapping | Precise VAT/SAT volumes; longitudinal tracking | Expensive; limited availability |
| **MRI spectroscopy (¹H-MRS)** | Adipose tissue triglyceride content | Gold standard for tissue-specific fat quantification | Small volume; expertise required |
| **MRI elastography** | Adipose tissue stiffness/fibrosis | Quantifies fibrosis | Limited validation in adipose tissue |

#### 6.3.2 DEXA (Dual-Energy X-ray Absorptiometry)

DEXA provides:
- **Total body fat mass and percentage**
- **Android/gynoid fat ratio** (android = visceral/abdominal; higher ratio = worse metabolic risk)
- **Trunk fat** (proxy for visceral adiposity)
- **Appendicular lean mass** (sarcopenia assessment)
- **Bone mineral density**

Limitations: Cannot distinguish visceral from subcutaneous fat directly; limited soft tissue resolution.

#### 6.3.3 CT (Computed Tomography)

CT (particularly multi-detector row CT) provides:
- **Volumetric VAT and SAT quantification** (cross-sectional or volumetric)
- **Liver attenuation (Hounsfield units)** → hepatic steatosis grading
- **Epicardial adipose tissue (EAT) volume** — highly associated with cardiovascular risk
- **Pericardial and perivascular adipose tissue** assessment

Limitations: Ionizing radiation; not suitable for repeated longitudinal assessments in healthy individuals.

#### 6.3.4 PET/CT with Brown Fat Imaging

**¹⁸F-FDG PET/CT** can quantify **brown adipose tissue (BAT) glucose uptake** — a functional measure of BAT thermogenic activity. With cold stimulation (cold water vest or cold air exposure), BAT activation can be assessed:

- BAT activity (measured as SUVmax — standardized uptake value) declines with age (from ~70% of young adults having detectable BAT to <10% in those >60 years)
- BAT SUVmax correlates inversely with BMI, visceral adiposity, and insulin resistance
- Therapeutic agents (β3-agonists, GLP-1 analogs, metformin) can increase BAT activity

#### 6.3.5 Ultrasound

| Modality | Application |
|----------|------------|
| **Conventional US** | Subcutaneous fat thickness; echogenicity (correlates with fibrosis) |
| **Shear wave elastography (SWE)** | Adipose tissue stiffness ( fibrosis); correlates with metabolic dysfunction |
| **Contrast-enhanced US (CEUS)** | Adipose tissue vascularization |

## 7. Drug Discovery Pipeline for Adipose Aging

### 7.1 Overview of the Drug Discovery Framework

The discovery of anti-aging therapeutics targeting adipose tissue follows a classical drug discovery pipeline adapted for the unique biology of aging:

```
Target Identification → Hit Discovery → Lead Optimization → Preclinical Validation → Clinical Development
     ↓                      ↓                  ↓                    ↓                       ↓
  Genomics/          HTS / in silico     Medicinal chemistry    In vivo models         Phase I/II/III
 proteomics/         phenotypic assays   ADMET optimization     (aged rodents,         Regulatory
 CRISPR screens                            PK/PD profiling       NHPs, humanized         approval
                                       Activity cliff          organoids)
```

### 7.2 Target Identification

#### 7.2.1 Genomic and Transcriptomic Approaches

**Bulk RNA-seq** of aged vs. young adipose tissue from humans and animal models has identified key differentially expressed genes:

| Gene | Change | Function | Therapeutic Relevance |
|------|--------|---------|----------------------|
| CDKN2A (p16INK4a) | ↑ 3–5 fold | Cell cycle inhibitor; senescence driver | Senolytic target |
| IL6 | ↑ 2–4 fold | Pro-inflammatory cytokine | SASP biomarker; JAK target |
| CCL2 | ↑ 2–3 fold | Monocyte chemoattractant | CCR2 antagonist target |
| SOD2 | ↑ (compensatory) | Mitochondrial antioxidant | Mitochondrial health |
| UCP1 | ↓ (in aged BAT) | Thermogenesis | Browning target |
| PPARG | ↓ in aged ASCs | Adipogenesis | PPAR-γ agonist target |
| SIRT1 | ↓ 30–50% | NAD⁺-dependent deacetylase | SIRT1 activator; NAD⁺ booster |
| FGF21 | ↑ (compensatory) | Metabolic regulator | FGF21 analog target |
| GDF15 | ↑ 2–5 fold | Stress-induced cytokine | GFRAL agonist target |

**Single-cell RNA-seq (scRNA-seq)** has been transformative in identifying specific cell type changes:
- **Adipocyte precursor populations**: Identification of distinct preadipocyte subpopulations (e.g., DPP4+ early precursors, ICAM1+ committed preadipocytes) that are differentially affected by aging
- **Senescent cell identification**: Identification of a "senescence-like" adipocyte state characterized by SASP gene co-expression
- **ATM heterogeneity**: Identification of M1-like, M2-like, and lipid-associated macrophage (LAM) subsets in aged adipose tissue
- **T-cell aging**: PD1+ exhausted T cells accumulate in aged adipose tissue

#### 7.2.2 Proteomic Approaches

**Quantitative proteomics** (TMT labeling, label-free DIA) of aged adipose tissue has identified:
- Upregulation of ECM proteins (COL1A1, COL3A1, FN1, LOX) → fibrosis
- Downregulation of mitochondrial proteins (Complex I-V subunits) → mitochondrial dysfunction
- Upregulation of inflammatory proteins (SASP factors)
- Accumulation of damaged/misfolded proteins → proteostasis loss
- Altered AMPK/mTOR pathway phosphorylation status

#### 7.2.3 Epigenomic Profiling

**ATAC-seq (Assay for Transposase-Accessible Chromatin sequencing)** identifies open chromatin regions — regulatory elements active in aged adipose tissue:
- Increased chromatin accessibility at inflammatory gene loci (NF-κB target genes)
- Reduced accessibility at adipogenic loci (PPARG, CEBPA)
- Altered enhancer landscapes in aged ASCs

**ChIP-seq** for histone modifications (H3K27ac, H3K9me3, H3K4me1) reveals altered enhancer activity at aging-related gene loci.

#### 7.2.4 CRISPR Screens for Anti-Senescence Targets

Genome-wide CRISPR-Cas9 knockout and CRISPRa (activation) screens in senescent cells have identified:
- **Pro-senescence genes** (required for senescence maintenance): MDM2, CDK2, BCL-XL — potential senolytic targets
- **Anti-senescence genes** (whose inhibition extends healthspan): PTPN14, TSC2, SIRT1
- **SASP regulators**: CYLD, BRD4, IKKβ
- **Senescence cell surface proteins**: HSP90AA1, ENPP1, CDCP1 — potential targeting strategies

### 7.4 Lead Optimization

#### 7.4.1 Structure-Activity Relationship (SAR) Studies

For senolytic candidates, lead optimization focuses on:

1. **Improving selectivity index** (toxicity to senescent vs. non-senescent cells) — the central challenge
2. **Optimizing pharmacokinetics** (half-life, oral bioavailability, tissue penetration)
3. **Improving safety profile** (reducing off-target kinase inhibition for dasatinib analogs; reducing thrombocytopenia forBCL-XL inhibitors)
4. **Establishing activity cliffs** — identifying critical structural modifications that dramatically change potency

**Activity cliff detection**: A key concept in medicinal chemistry, where small structural changes cause disproportionate changes in activity (e.g., methylation of quercetin → significant change in senolytic potency). High-throughput SAR data analyzed using computational activity cliff detection algorithms can accelerate lead optimization.

### 7.6 Biomarker-Driven Clinical Trial Design

Modern clinical trials for anti-aging adipose therapies incorporate:

1. **Baseline biomarker enrichment**: Selecting patients with elevated SASP markers (IL-6 >2 pg/mL, GDF15 >1,000 pg/mL) or elevated senescence biomarkers to enrich for the target population

2. **Pharmacodynamic biomarkers**: Measuring changes in adipose tissue biomarkers (via subcutaneous adipose biopsies) before and after treatment:
   - p16INK4a expression (RT-qPCR, flow cytometry)
   - SASP factor levels
   - Adipogenic gene expression (PPARG, CEBPA)
   - Senescence-associated chromatin changes

3. **Imaging endpoints**: Using MRI, DEXA, and PET-CT for non-invasive adipose tissue assessment at baseline and follow-up

4. **Composite endpoints**: Combining physical function (SPPB, 6MWT), metabolic (HOMA-IR), and inflammatory (hsCRP, IL-6) endpoints to capture holistic healthspan improvements

## 8. Future Directions

### 8.1 Multi-Target Approaches

Given the multifactorial nature of adipose tissue aging — involving senescence, mitochondrial dysfunction, autophagy impairment, inflammaging, and stem cell decline — **single-target interventions are unlikely to fully address the complexity of the aging phenotype**. Multi-target drug design is therefore a critical direction:

#### 8.1.1 Single-Molecule Multi-Target Drugs (Polypharmacology)

### 8.2 Combination Therapies

#### 8.2.1 Senolytics + Metabolic Agents

The combination of **senolytics with GLP-1 receptor agonists** represents a particularly promising approach:
- **Senolytics** reduce the burden of senescent adipocytes (source of SASP, inflammation)
- **GLP-1 agonists** improve metabolic function and reduce appetite (lifestyle modification support)
- **Preclinical synergy**: D+Q + semaglutide in obese mice showed superior weight loss and metabolic improvement vs. either agent alone

#### 8.2.2 Senolytics + NAD⁺ Boosters

**NAD⁺ boosters (NMN, NR)** + **senolytics** could be a powerful rejuvenation combination:
- Senolytics eliminate the most damaged, aged cells
- NAD⁺ boosters restore metabolic function in the remaining (less aged) cell population
- SIRT1 activation by NAD⁺ boosters complements senolytic action

#### 8.2.3 Autophagy Induction + Senolytics

**mTOR inhibitors** (rapamycin, everolimus) or **AMPK activators** (metformin) combined with senolytics could enhance the clearance of senescent cell populations while also restoring autophagic flux in surviving cells.

### 8.3 Personalized Medicine

The field is moving toward **precision anti-aging therapeutics** for adipose tissue:

#### 8.3.1 Biomarker-Driven Patient Selection

| Biomarker | Patient Population | Recommended Therapy |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| High p16INK4a in adipose biopsy | Elevated senescence burden | Senolytic (D+Q, fisetin) |
| High GDF15 (>1,500 pg/mL) | Mitochondrial stress; cachexia risk | GDF15 receptor agonist (mazdutide) |
| Low adiponectin + high leptin | Severe insulin resistance | PPAR-γ agonist (pioglitazone) |
| Low NAD⁺ (muscle/adipose) | Sirtuin dysfunction | NAD⁺ booster (NMN, NR) |
| High IL-6 (>3 pg/mL) + high CRP | Chronic inflammation | JAK inhibitor (baricitinib) + metformin |
| Low UCP1 expression in SAT | Poor beige adipocyte capacity | FGF21 analog; β3-agonist |

#### 8.3.2 Pharmacogenomic Approaches

Genetic variants in key aging-related genes influence drug response:
- **PPARG Pro12Ala polymorphism**: Affects response to TZDs
- **FTO (fat mass and obesity-associated) gene variants**: Associated with response to GLP-1 agonists
- **SIRT1 polymorphisms**: Affect response to resveratrol and NAD⁺ boosters
- **MC4R variants**: Influence appetite and weight loss responses to GLP-1 agents

#### 8.3.3 Adipose Tissue "Omics" Profiling

A future clinical approach would involve:
1. **Subcutaneous adipose biopsy** at baseline
2. **Multi-omics profiling**: Transcriptomics (RNA-seq), proteomics, epigenomics (DNAm), metabolomics
3. **AI/ML integration** of multi-omic data to generate patient-specific "adipose aging signatures"
4. **Personalized therapeutic recommendation** based on the dominant aging mechanism

### 8.4 Systems Aging Biology and Network Pharmacology

A systems-level approach to adipose tissue aging integrates:
- **Intercellular signaling networks** (adipocyte-immune-stromal crosstalk)
- **Organelle-level dysfunction** (mitochondria-ER-lipid droplet axis)
- **Systemic hormones and cytokines** (adipokines, FGF21, GDF15, IGF-1)
- **Neural regulation** (sympathetic innervation of adipose tissue; hypothalamic-adipose axis)

**Network pharmacology** tools (e.g., STITCH, STRING, KEGG) can identify key hub genes and pathways that serve as master regulators of adipose aging — potential " Achilles heel" drug targets.

### 8.5 Emerging Research Frontiers

1. **Senolytic vaccines**: Targeting senescent cell surface antigens (e.g., uPAR) with therapeutic vaccines to generate anti-senescent immune responses — highly experimental but promising
2. **CAR-T cell therapy for senescence**: Engineering chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T cells targeting senescent cell surface proteins — preclinical validation ongoing
3. **Extracellular vesicle-based therapies**: Using EVs from young adipose-derived stromal cells (ASC-EVs) to rejuvenate aged adipocytes — early clinical trials
4. **Microbiome-adipose axis**: Gut dysbiosis → increased gut permeability → endotoxemia → adipose inflammation; targeting the microbiome with prebiotics, probiotics, or postbiotics as anti-aging interventions
5. **Mechanical stimulation**: Novel approaches using mechanotransduction (e.g., subcutaneous vibration, shear stress) to promote beige adipogenesis and reduce fibrosis
6. **Cold therapy and brown adipose tissue activation**: Whole-body cryotherapy, mild cold exposure, and pharmacologic BAT activation as metabolic interventions
7. **Adipose tissue cryopreservation and autologous transplantation**: Banking young adipose tissue for later autologous transplantation to restore tissue function
