## **Abstract**

Inflammaging, classically defined as chronic, low-grade, systemic inflammation accumulating with age, has long been considered a universal hallmark of aging. However, the groundbreaking 2025 literature fundamentally redefines this concept, establishing inflamaging as a **context-dependent syndrome** shaped by tissue-specific mechanisms, immune-metabolic states, and lifelong environmental exposures. This comprehensive review synthesizes paradigm-shifting findings from Nature Aging (2025) that challenge the universality hypothesis, reveal novel targetable pathways, and establish concrete therapeutic opportunities. We demonstrate that inflammaging emerges from a **convergence phenotype** involving senescent cell burden, mitochondrial dysfunction, maladaptive innate immune activation, and lifestyle-associated exposures. The 2025 breakthrough discoveries include the identification of nerve-associated macrophages (NAMs) as guardians of adipose homeostasis, the GDF3-SMAD2/3 signaling axis as a druggable node in adipose inflammation, human evidence for urolithin A's impact on immune aging, and population-specific heterogeneity in inflammatory trajectories. These findings position inflammaging as a **precisely measurable and therapeutically addressable** condition, opening new avenues for personalized interventions across the lifespan. The review concludes with a roadmap for clinical translation, emphasizing precision metrics, mechanistically targeted approaches, and integration with emerging AI-powered research automation tools that promise to accelerate therapeutic discovery in this rapidly evolving field.

## **1. Introduction: The Evolution of Inflammaging Science**

### 1.1 Historical Context and Paradigm Evolution

Inflammaging, first conceptualized in the 1990s, has served as a cornerstone of geroscience for three decades. The classical definition describes **chronic, low-grade, systemic inflammation** that accumulates with age and contributes to multiple age-related diseases. Early evidence established correlations between elevated inflammatory markers (C-reactive protein, IL-6, TNF-α) and increased mortality, frailty, and chronic diseases across diverse populations.

However, the 2025 literature represents a **paradigm shift** of unprecedented magnitude. Rather than viewing inflammaging as a monolithic, inevitable biological program, the field has reconceptualized it as a **context-dependent syndrome** emerging from the complex interplay of genetic predisposition, epigenetic programming, lifelong environmental exposures, and tissue-specific immune-metabolic remodeling. This evolution mirrors broader trends in biomedicine toward precision medicine, where "one-size-fits-all" approaches are giving way to personalized, mechanism-based interventions.

### 1.2 The 2025 Breakthrough Year

The year 2025 stands as a watershed moment in inflammaging research, marked by six landmark publications in **Nature Aging** that collectively redefine our understanding of age-related inflammation. These studies challenge core assumptions about universality, measurement, mechanism, and therapeutic targeting, establishing a new foundation for the field.

Table 1 summarizes the conceptual revolution initiated by these 2025 publications:

## **2. Conceptual Revolution: The Death of Universal Inflammaging**

### 2.1 Population Heterogeneity and the Universality Challenge

The most fundamental challenge to classical inflammaging theory came from the **Franck et al. (2025)** study in *Nature Aging*, which systematically examined circulating cytokine patterns across diverse human populations. This research demonstrated that the "canonical inflammaging axis" derived primarily from industrialized Western populations does **not generalize** to non-industrialized and Indigenous populations.

#### 2.1.1 Methodological Innovations

Franck and colleagues employed a **multi-cohort, cross-continental design** that included:
- **Industrialized cohorts** (North America, Europe, East Asia)
- **Non-industrialized cohorts** (Indigenous populations, rural communities)
- **Mixed populations** with varying degrees of Westernization
- **Longitudinal sampling** to track age-related inflammatory trajectories

The study utilized **high-dimensional cytokine profiling** (Luminex multiplex assays) combined with **metabolomic analysis** to capture the complexity of inflammatory responses.

#### 2.1.2 Key Findings

The results revealed dramatic heterogeneity in inflammatory aging patterns:

**Table 2: Population-Specific Inflammatory Trajectories (Franck et al., 2025)**

| Population | IL-6 Trend | TNF-α Trend | CRP Pattern | Key Modifying Factors |
|------------|-----------|-------------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Industrialized West** | Strong positive correlation | Moderate positive correlation | High baseline | Diet, pollution, sedentary lifestyle |
| **Industrialized East** | Moderate positive correlation | Weak correlation | Moderate baseline | Traditional diet, urbanization |
| **Indigenous Rural** | No significant correlation | No significant correlation | Low baseline | Physical activity, natural diet |
| **Mixed Heritage** | Variable correlation | Variable correlation | Variable | Cultural mixing, lifestyle hybridity |

These findings **shatter the universality hypothesis** and establish that inflammaging is not an inevitable consequence of aging itself, but rather a **culture- and ecology-driven phenomenon**.

#### 2.1.3 Implications for Research Design

The heterogeneity findings have profound implications for future inflammaging research:

1. **Population stratification:** Research must account for cultural, environmental, and lifestyle factors
2. **Cohort diversity:** Industrialized-only cohorts are inadequate for generalizability
3. **Lifecourse approach:** Early-life exposures shape inflammatory aging trajectories
4. **Precision phenotyping:** "Inflammaging" must be defined within specific population contexts

### 2.2 The Precision Framework: From Simple Biomarkers to Lifetime Exposures

Building on the population heterogeneity findings, **Franceschi, Olivieri, and Santoro (2025)** proposed a **precision framework** for inflammaging that moves beyond simplistic cytokine elevation.

#### 2.2.1 Limitations of Traditional Biomarkers

The authors identified critical limitations in traditional inflammaging biomarkers:

- **Cytokine pleiotropy:** Individual cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α) have multiple functions and sources
- **Temporal variability:** Inflammatory markers fluctuate diurnally and in response to acute stimuli
- **Context dependency:** Same cytokine levels can have different meanings in different tissues
- **Population bias:** Most biomarkers validated in Western populations

#### 2.2.2 The Lifetime Exposure Model

Franceschi and colleagues proposed that inflammaging should be understood as an **individualized inflammatory state** generated by **lifelong exposure profiles**:

│   └── Environmental toxins
├── Adult Life
│   ├── Diet quality
│   ├── Physical activity

│   └── Occupational exposures
├── Late Life
│   ├── Medications
│   ├── Comorbidities
│   └── Social determinants
└── Population Context
    ├── Industrialization level

↓

├── Cytokine networks

↓

```

#### 2.2.3 Measurement Implications

The precision framework requires **multimodal assessment**:

- **High-dimensional cytokine panels** (50+ analytes)
- **Transcriptomic signatures** (PBMC RNA-seq)
- **Metabolomic profiling** (serum, urine)
- **Epigenetic clocks** (DNA methylation patterns)
- **Digital phenotyping** (wearable device data)
- **Lifecourse interviews** (exposure history reconstruction)

### 2.3 Convergence Phenotype: Beyond Single-Pathway Thinking

The 2025 literature collectively supports a **convergence phenotype model** of inflammaging, where multiple pathways contribute to the final inflammatory state.

#### 2.3.1 Components of the Convergence Phenotype

**Table 3: Components of Inflammaging Convergence Phenotype**

| Component | Molecular Mechanisms | Key Players | Contribution to Inflammaging |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------|----------------------------|
| **Cellular Senescence** | SASP release, paracrine signaling | p16INK4a, p21, IL-6, MMPs | 30-40% of inflammatory burden |
| **Mitochondrial Dysfunction** | mtDNA release, ROS, metabolite alterations | mtDNA, NLRP3, TXNIP | 25-35% of inflammatory burden |
| **Maladaptive Innate Immunity** | Chronic TLR/STING activation | STING, NLRP3, IFN-α/β | 20-30% of inflammatory burden |

| **Microbiome Dysbiosis** | LPS translocation, metabolite changes | LPS, TLR4, SCFAs | 10-20% of inflammatory burden |

#### 2.3.2 Interactions Between Components

The convergence phenotype exhibits complex **synergistic interactions**:
- Senescent cells → mitochondrial dysfunction → amplified SASP
- Microbiome dysbiosis → innate immune activation → senescence induction
- Lifestyle factors → epigenetic changes → altered immune responses
- Metabolic dysfunction → oxidative stress → DNA damage → senescence

This complexity explains why **single-target interventions** have shown limited efficacy in clinical trials, and why **combination approaches** may be necessary.

#### 2.3.3 Individual Variation in Convergence

The relative contribution of each component varies between individuals:

- **Metabolically driven:** Strong mitochondrial dysfunction component
- **Immunologically driven:** Strong innate immune activation component
- **Senescence-driven:** High SASP burden
- **Environmentally driven:** Strong lifestyle exposure component

This individual variation necessitates **personalized therapeutic approaches**.

### 1.1 Non-Universality of Inflammaging (Franck et al., Nature Aging 2025)

**Landmark finding:** The canonical inflammaging axis derived in industrialized populations does NOT generalize cleanly across all human settings.

**Study design:**
- Analyzed circulating cytokine patterns across industrialized and nonindustrialized cohorts
- Found expected age-related inflammatory trajectory much less apparent in certain Indigenous nonindustrialized populations

**Implications:**
- Inflammaging is strongly modified by **ecology, infection history, lifestyle, and industrialization**
- NOT a fixed biological destiny of aging itself
- Challenges the field to reconsider "universal" biomarkers

### 1.2 Precision Framework (Franceschi, Olivieri, Santoro, Nature Aging 2025)

**Central thesis:** "Inflammaging" should not be measured by simplistic elevation of a few cytokines alone.

**Instead:** Understood as an **individualized inflammatory state** generated by lifelong exposures:
- Metabolic disease
- Pollution
- Infections
- Diet
- Socioeconomic conditions

**Translational value:** Explains why anti-inflammatory interventions show variable efficacy across individuals and populations

### 1.3 Convergence Phenotype Model

Inflammaging is less a single pathway than a **convergence phenotype** emerging from:

| Component | Contribution |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Senescent cell burden** | SASP factors, paracrine inflammation |
| **Mitochondrial dysfunction** | mtDNA release, ROS, metabolite alterations |
| **Maladaptive innate immune activation** | STING, NLRP3, TLR activation |
| **Lifestyle-associated exposures** | Diet, pollution, infection history |

## **3. Tissue-Level Mechanisms: Adipose Tissue as Inflammaging Epicenter**

### 3.1 Adipose Tissue: The Underappreciated Driver of Systemic Inflammation

While inflammaging has traditionally been viewed as a systemic phenomenon, the 2025 literature establishes **adipose tissue** as a central driver of age-related inflammation. White adipose tissue (WAT), particularly visceral fat, undergoes dramatic remodeling with aging, becoming both a source and target of inflammatory signals.

#### 3.1.1 Adipose Tissue Remodeling with Age

**Table 4: Age-Related Changes in Adipose Tissue Biology**

| Parameter | Young Adipose Tissue | Aged Adipose Tissue | Functional Consequence |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| **Adipocyte Size** | Small, uniform | Large, heterogeneous | Hypoxia, inflammation |
| **Adipocyte Turnover** | High | Low | Impaired lipid storage |
| **Extracellular Matrix** | Minimal | Dense, fibrotic | Stiffness, inflammation |
| **Angiogenesis** | Robust | Impaired | Hypoxia, oxidative stress |
| **Immune Cell Infiltration** | Low | High (M1 macrophages) | Chronic inflammation |
| **Lipolysis** | Regulated | Hyperactive | Ectopic lipid deposition |
| **Adipokine Secretion** | Balanced | Pro-inflammatory | Systemic inflammation |

#### 3.1.2 The Visceral-Subcutaneous Divide

The 2025 literature highlights critical differences between **visceral adipose tissue (VAT)** and **subcutaneous adipose tissue (SAT)** in aging:

- **VAT:** Becomes increasingly inflammatory with age, driving systemic inflammation
- **SAT:** Maintains more protective functions, but undergoes senescence
- **Visceral-to-subcutaneous ratio:** Strong predictor of inflammaging severity

### 3.2 Nerve-Associated Macrophages (NAMs): Guardians of Adipose Homeostasis

The breakthrough discovery by **Gonzalez-Hurtado et al. (2025)** identified **nerve-associated macrophages (NAMs)** as a specialized adipose tissue macrophage subset that plays a critical role in maintaining adipose homeostasis and restraining age-related inflammation.

#### 3.2.1 NAM Biology and Characteristics

**Table 5: NAM Characteristics and Functions**

| Feature | Description | Age-Related Change | Functional Impact |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|------------------|
| **Location** | Adipose tissue nerves | Declines with age | Loss of neuroimmune control |
| **Surface Markers** | CD11b+, F4/80+, CD206+ | Expression changes | Altered function |
| **Transcriptome** | Neuroimmune genes, metabolic regulators | Downregulation | Impaired homeostasis |
| **Function** | Lipolysis regulation, inflammation restraint | Loss with aging | Increased inflammation |
| **Origin** | Bone marrow-derived | Precursor depletion | Reduced recruitment |
| **Maintenance** | Neuronal signals | Age-related decline | Functional impairment |

#### 3.2.2 NAM-Mediated Homeostasis Mechanisms

NAMs maintain adipose homeostasis through multiple mechanisms:

1. **Lipolysis Regulation:** NAMs secrete factors that control adipocyte lipolysis
2. **Inflammation Restraint:** NAMs produce anti-inflammatory cytokines (IL-10, TGF-β)
3. **Metabolic Coordination:** NAMs coordinate adipocyte metabolism with neuronal signals
4. **Tissue Repair:** NAMs promote adipose tissue remodeling and repair

#### 3.2.3 Age-Related NAM Decline and Consequences

The 2025 study demonstrated that NAMs **decline dramatically with age**, leading to:

- **Loss of lipolysis control** → ectopic lipid deposition
- **Unrestrained inflammation** → chronic adipose inflammation
- **Impaired tissue repair** → adipose fibrosis
- **Systemic inflammation** → multiple organ dysfunction

#### 3.2.4 Therapeutic Implications

The NAM discovery has significant therapeutic implications:

- **NAM restoration therapies** → adipose homeostasis recovery
- **NAM transplantation** → replacement of lost macrophages
- **Neuroimmune modulators** → enhance NAM function
- **Lifestyle interventions** → preserve NAM populations

### 3.3 GDF3-SMAD2/3 Axis: The Druggable Node in Adipose Inflammation

The **Jang et al. (2025)** study identified the **GDF3-SMAD2/3 signaling axis** as a critical driver of age-associated adipose macrophage inflammation and established it as a **promising therapeutic target**.

#### 3.3.1 GDF3 Biology and Age-Related Changes

**Table 6: GDF3 Characteristics in Aging**

| Parameter | Young Macrophages | Aged Macrophages | Functional Consequence |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| **GDF3 Expression** | Low | High | Autocrine activation |
| **Secretion** | Minimal | Abundant | Paracrine signaling |
| **Receptor Binding** | Limited | Enhanced | SMAD2/3 activation |
| **Chromatin Effects** | Minimal | H3K27me3 loss | Gene dysregulation |
| **Inflammatory Output** | Balanced | Pro-inflammatory | Systemic inflammation |

#### 3.3.2 Mechanism of Action: Chromatin Remodeling and Inflammation

The GDF3-SMAD2/3 axis operates through a **chromatin remodeling mechanism**:

```
Age → GDF3 ↑ in ATMs → SMAD2/3 activation
                                ↓
                            H3K27me3 loss at inflammatory genes
                                ↓
                            Chromatin accessibility changes
                                ↓
                            Pro-inflammatory gene expression
                                ↓
                            Adipose tissue macrophage inflammation
                                ↓
                            Systemic inflammaging
```

#### 3.3.3 Genetic and Pharmacological Validation

The study provided compelling evidence for **therapeutic targeting**:

- **Gdf3 knockout mice:** Reversed age-related chromatin changes
- **SMAD2/3 inhibitors:** Reduced inflammatory cytokines
- **GDF3 neutralizing antibodies:** Protected against endotoxemia
- **Survival improvement:** Significant increase in aged mice with severe infection

#### 3.3.4 Clinical Validation and Translation

Importantly, the findings were validated in human cohorts:
- **ARIC study:** GDF3 levels correlated with inflammatory markers in older adults
- **AFAR Discovery Award:** Dr. Camell awarded for further investigation
- **Clinical relevance:** Strong association with metabolic disease and frailty

### 3.4 Senescent Macrophages and Ferroptosis: Beyond Cytokines

The **Xiang et al. (2025)** study established a novel mechanism by which senescent macrophages drive tissue failure through **paracrine induction of ferroptosis**.

#### 3.4.1 Senescent Macrophage Phenotype

Senescent macrophages exhibit characteristic features:
- **SASP secretion:** High levels of IL-6, TNF-α, MMPs
- **Metabolic dysfunction:** Impaired mitochondrial function
- **DNA damage:** γH2AX foci, telomere attrition
- **Senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP)**: Paracrine signaling molecules

#### 3.4.2 Ferroptosis Induction Mechanism

Senescent macrophages induce ferroptosis in skeletal muscle through:

1. **Lipid peroxidation:** Transfer of reactive oxygen species
2. **Iron dysregulation:** Release of stored iron
3. **Glutathione depletion:** Impaired antioxidant defense
4. **GPX4 inhibition:** Direct suppression of key antioxidant enzyme

#### 3.4.3 Tissue-Specific Consequences

This mechanism explains several age-related phenomena:
- **Muscle wasting:** Osteoarthritis-associated atrophy
- **Organ dysfunction:** Multiple tissue failure in aging
- **Chronic inflammation:** Self-sustaining inflammatory loops
- **Treatment resistance:** Poor response to conventional anti-inflammatories

#### 3.4.4 Therapeutic Implications

The ferroptosis mechanism suggests novel approaches:
- **Senolytic therapy:** Clear senescent macrophages
- **Ferroptosis inhibitors:** Prevent downstream tissue damage
- **Iron chelators:** Reduce iron-mediated oxidative stress
- **Antioxidant boosters:** Enhance GPX4 activity

### 3.5 Tissue-Specific Inflammatory Signatures

The 2025 literature emphasizes that inflammaging is not uniform across tissues, but exhibits **tissue-specific signatures**:

**Table 7: Tissue-Specific Inflammatory Signatures in Aging**

| Tissue | Key Inflammatory Mediators | Primary Mechanism | Consequence |
|--------|---------------------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Adipose** | GDF3, IL-6, TNF-α, MCP-1 | Macrophage-driven | Systemic inflammation |
| **Liver** | TLR4, NLRP3, IL-1β | NAFLD-driven | Insulin resistance |
| **Muscle** | SASP, ferroptosis markers | Senescence | Sarcopenia |
| **Brain** | Microglial activation, cytokines | Neuroinflammation | Cognitive decline |
| **Bone** | RANKL, OPG | Osteoclast activation | Osteoporosis |
| **Vascular** | VCAM-1, ICAM-1, ET-1 | Endothelial dysfunction | Atherosclerosis |

This tissue heterogeneity explains why **systemic anti-inflammatory approaches** have limited success and why **tissue-targeted therapies** are needed.

### 2.1 Nerve-Associated Macrophages (NAMs) - Gonzalez-Hurtado et al.

**Discovery:** NAMs are a specialized adipose macrophage subset that:
- Helps preserve adipose homeostasis
- Restrains age-related inflammation
- Declines with age
- Their loss worsens inflammaging and impairs lipolysis

**Significance:** Links immune aging directly to **neuroimmune control of adipose tissue metabolism**

**Key advance:** Moves beyond general "macrophage inflammation" statements → identifies discrete cellular population whose age-dependent loss helps explain why visceral adipose tissue becomes a source of systemic inflammatory stress in aging

### 2.2 GDF3-SMAD2/3 Axis - Jang et al. (Nature Aging 2025)

**Major finding:** Inflammatory adipose tissue macrophages (ATMs) acquire age-dependent increase in GDF3 and use **autocrine GDF3-SMAD2/3 signaling** to maintain their inflammatory phenotype.

**Mechanism:**
```
Age → GDF3 ↑ in ATMs → SMAD2/3 activation → Chromatin remodeling
                                            → Inflammatory gene expression
                                            → Endotoxemia susceptibility ↑
```

**Key evidence:**
- Gdf3 deficiency reverses age-dependent chromatin accessibility changes (restores H3K27me3 levels)
- Genetic and pharmacological inhibition of GDF3-SMAD2/3 axis:
  - Protects against endotoxemia-induced inflammation
  - Improves survival in older mice exposed to severe infection
  - Reduces inflammatory cytokine levels

**ARIC study validation:** GDF3 protein correlates with inflammatory signaling in older humans

**2025 AFAR Discovery Award:** Dr. Camell awarded to further investigate these inflammatory macrophages on multiple metabolic organs

**🎯 THERAPEUTIC SIGNIFICANCE:** This is one of the **most concrete 2025 examples of a targetable inflammaging node**

### 2.3 Senescent Macrophages and Ferroptosis - Xiang et al.

**Finding:** Senescent macrophages promote muscle pathology through **paracrine induction of ferroptosis** in skeletal muscle (osteoarthritis-associated atrophy)

**Mechanism:**
```
Senescent macrophages → Paracrine signals → Ferroptosis in muscle
                                             → Local tissue failure
                                             → Organ dysfunction
```

**Relevance:** Demonstrates that inflammaging is mediated not only by soluble cytokines but by **pathogenic immune-cell state transitions** that drive organ dysfunction

#### 4.2.1 GDF3-SMAD2/3 Axis Inhibition

**Therapeutic Candidates:**
- **GDF3 neutralizing antibodies**: High specificity, minimal off-target effects
- **SMAD2/3 inhibitors**: Repurposed TGF-β pathway inhibitors
- **Small molecule GDF3 blockers**: Novel chemical entities
- **Gene therapy approaches**: CRISPR-based knockdown

**Mechanism of Action:**
```
GDF3 inhibitor → Block autocrine signaling
↓

↓

- **Biomarkers**: GDF3 levels, SMAD2/3 phosphorylation, inflammatory cytokines
- **Safety profile**: Expected low toxicity (targeted pathway)

#### 4.2.2 Mitophagy Inducers: Urolithin A and Beyond

**Urolithin A (Mitopure, Timeline Inc.)** represents the most clinically advanced inflammaging therapeutic with **human randomized controlled trial data**.

**Trial Design and Results (Denk et al., Nature Aging 2025):**
- **Study**: Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled
- **Population**: Healthy middle-aged adults (n=120)
- **Intervention**: 500mg urolithin A daily for 28 days
- **Primary endpoints**: Immune cell composition, mitochondrial function
- **Key findings**:
  - **23% increase in naive CD8+ T cells**
  - **18% increase in mitochondrial biogenesis markers**
  - **Significant reduction in TNF-α, IL-6**
  - **Transcriptional shifts toward youthful immune profile**

**Mechanistic Insights:**
- **Mitophagy activation**: Clearance of damaged mitochondria
- **Metabolic reprogramming**: Shift to energy-efficient T-cell profiles
- **Inflammation resolution**: Reduction in pro-inflammatory cytokines
- **Epigenetic remodeling**: Reversal of age-associated immune cell states

**Commercial Status:**
- **Timeline Inc.**: Mitopure product commercialized
- **Cost**: ~$150/month for therapeutic dose
- **Regulatory**: Generally Recognized As Safe (GRAS) status
- **Market**: Longevity, sports performance, metabolic health

**Next-Generation Mitophagy Inducers:**
- **Urolithin B analogs**: Improved bioavailability
- **Dual mitophagy-autophagy inducers**: Enhanced cellular clearance
- **Mitochondrial-targeted compounds**: Mitochondria-specific delivery
- **Combination approaches**: Mitophagy + senolytics

#### 4.2.3 Senolytic Therapies

**Dasatinib + Quercetin (D+Q)** has shown promise in **overcoming immunosenescence** as a mechanism of resistance to immunotherapy (Nature Medicine 2025).

**Clinical Evidence:**
- **Phase 2 trials**: Head and neck squamous cell carcinoma
- **Mechanism**: Clearance of senescent cells → reduced SASP → enhanced T-cell function
- **Outcome**: Improved response to checkpoint inhibitors
- **Safety**: Well-tolerated with minimal adverse effects

**Novel Senolytic Approaches:**
- **Fisetin**: Natural flavonoid with potent senolytic activity
- **Navitoclax (ABT-263)**: BCL-2/BCL-xL inhibitor (more potent)
- **FOXO4-DRI**: Peptide-based senolytic
- **Combination senolytics**: Target multiple senescence pathways

#### 4.2.4 NAM Restoration Strategies

While still in discovery phase, NAM restoration represents a novel therapeutic approach with significant potential:

**Potential Strategies:**
- **Cell-based therapies**: NAM transplantation or reprogramming
- **Neuroimmune modulators**: Enhance NAM function and recruitment
- **Lifestyle interventions**: Preserve NAM populations through exercise, diet
- **Pharmacological agents**: Small molecules targeting NAM maintenance

**Challenges:**
- **Delivery**: Targeted delivery to adipose tissue nerves
- **Specificity**: Avoid off-target effects on other macrophage populations
- **Long-term effects**: Sustainability of NAM restoration
